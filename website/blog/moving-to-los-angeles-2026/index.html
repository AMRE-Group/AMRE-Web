Moving to Los Angeles: the honest relocation guide.

LA isn't one city — it's dozens of small ones stitched together by freeways. Here's what it really costs to live here in 2026, how to pick the neighborhood that fits your life, and a month-by-month plan to land well.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · June 22, 2026 · 8 min read

Almost everyone who moves to Los Angeles arrives with a picture in their head — palm trees, an ocean breeze, a life that feels a little more open. The good news: that picture is real. The honest part: LA will hand it to you on its own terms, and the people who land well are the ones who understand the city before they sign anything.

Here's the thing most relocation guides miss: Los Angeles isn't one market — it's dozens of small cities wearing one name. The version of LA you'll love (and afford) depends entirely on which of those small cities you choose.

What it actually costs.

There's no soft way to say it: LA is expensive. Overall cost of living runs about 50% above the national average, and housing does most of the lifting. As of mid-2026, the median home sale price in the city is roughly $1.0 million — though that single number hides enormous range, from sub-$700K condos in the urban core to multi-million-dollar homes in the hills.

But context matters. If you're coming from the Bay Area — and a lot of people are; San Francisco is the metro whose home-searchers eye LA more than any other — these numbers can feel like relief, not sticker shock. We wrote the full comparison in moving to LA from San Francisco.

Pick your LA, not just a house.

This is the single most important move you'll make. Choose the right neighborhood and almost everything else gets easier — commute, budget, weekends, the whole feel of your day. Choose wrong and even a beautiful house can feel like the wrong life. Start from how you want to live, then work back to the map:

  • Beach & walkable — Santa Monica, Venice, Playa del Rey · entry ~$1.4M+ · guides: Santa Monica, Playa del Rey
  • Views & privacy — Bel Air, Hollywood Hills · entry ~$2.0M+ · guide: Bel Air
  • Family & space — Sherman Oaks, Studio City (the Valley) · entry ~$1.3M+ · guide: Sherman Oaks
  • Culture & value — Mid-City, West Adams · entry ~$1.0M+ · guide: Mid-City
  • Urban & nightlife — DTLA, Koreatown, Silver Lake · condos from ~$700K

Typical entry prices approximate, mid-2026, and vary widely by street and property type. Anchor on lifestyle first, price second.

The most common transplant mistake: signing a 12-month lease in the first neighborhood you tour, before you understand the city. Give yourself room to learn LA before you lock in.

Rent first, or buy now?

If you don't yet know the city, renting for six to twelve months is a smart, low-risk way to test-drive neighborhoods before you commit hundreds of thousands of dollars. If you already know exactly where you want to be and plan to stay several years, buying sooner can make sense — LA home prices have appreciated about 5.3% a year over the last 35 years despite two major downturns. How much you need to earn.

At a roughly $1.0 million median price and 2026 mortgage rates, comfortably buying in the city generally calls for a household income in the low-to-mid six figures plus a meaningful down payment. The good news: widening your search to LA County, or to condos and townhomes, lowers that bar substantially. A local buyer's agent can map your real number to the neighborhoods where it goes furthest.

Key takeaways

  • ~$1.0M median home price in the city of LA, mid-2026 — from sub-$700K condos to hillside estates.
  • Pick the neighborhood first: beach, views, family space, culture-and-value, or urban — each is a different LA.
  • Rent 6–12 months if you don't know the city; buy sooner if you know exactly where you want to be.
  • Follow the sequence: explore → visit → pre-approve → tour → land. Prepared beats fast.
