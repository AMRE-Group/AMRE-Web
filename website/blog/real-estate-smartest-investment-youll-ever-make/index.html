Not just a roof: the smartest investment you'll ever make?

Every smart financial move requires sacrifice — but real estate is different. It leverages your money in ways stocks and 401(k)s can't, with appreciation, tax benefits, and a place to live built in. Here's the 10-year math.

The 10-year scenario.

Take a $600,000 property with 20% down ($120,000), a $480,000 mortgage at 6%, and 8% annual appreciation. After ten years the property is worth roughly $1.3M — and between appreciation and principal paydown, you've built about $665,000 in equity. The same $120,000 in other vehicles tells a very different story:

Why leverage changes everything.

In every other option, your returns compound only on the cash you invested. In real estate, 8% appreciation applies to the full $600,000 — a $48,000 first-year gain on your $120,000 outlay. Stocks may post higher percentage returns, but they can't match leveraged dollar returns in an appreciating market.

You're building wealth on the property's total value — not just your down payment. That's the entire game.

The honest costs.

The gains aren't free. Over ten years on this property, budget roughly $60,000 in property tax (~1%/yr), $6,000 in insurance (~0.1%/yr), and $6,000+ in maintenance — call it ~$72,000. Even net of those costs, the equity outcome roughly doubles the best-performing alternative. And unlike the alternatives, you lived in the investment the whole time.

Beyond the numbers.

Real estate adds advantages the brokerage account can't: potential deductions for mortgage interest and property taxes, rental-income potential — the engine behind duplex ownership strategies — and a natural inflation hedge, since both values and rents tend to rise with prices. Our 35-year LA appreciation study shows how durable that compounding has been, and our homeownership benefits guide covers the rest.

Why real estate often wins

  • Leverage: borrowed money amplifies returns on your actual cash.
  • Appreciation: gains accrue on the entire property value.
  • Equity building: every payment converts debt into ownership.
  • Tax benefits: potential deductions for interest, taxes, and expenses.
  • Income + inflation hedge: rents and values typically rise together.

Frequently asked questions.

Is real estate better than stocks?

Unleveraged, stocks often win on percentage returns. Leveraged, real estate usually wins on dollars built — at the cost of liquidity and concentration. Most wealthy households hold both.

Is renting throwing money away?

Renting buys real flexibility — but $3,000/month is $360,000 over a decade with no asset at the end. We ran the LA-specific version in rent vs. own at $1M.

What about estate and tax planning?

Ownership opens doors renting can't — trusts, 1031 exchanges, and step-up strategies. Start with our estate planning guide for property owners.
