The Market Is Moving — Just Not Always Where You Can See It.

Pricing discipline, a July 1 transfer-tax shift, and a surge in off-market activity are reshaping how Los Angeles real estate deals get done heading into mid-2026. Here's a clear-eyed read on each.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · March 17, 2026 · 5 min read

The Los Angeles market heading into mid-year isn't one market. It's two, operating in parallel, and which one you're in depends almost entirely on one thing: pricing. Well-priced, thoughtfully presented homes are moving quickly and often competitively — multiple offers, short market time, deals that close near or above list. Meanwhile, anything that came to market with an aspirational number is sitting — accumulating days and eventually requiring reductions that tend to cost more than the initial gap they were trying to bridge.

The pricing split: why one number changes everything.

Buyers in 2026 are sophisticated. They've spent months tracking price reductions and days on market, comparing active inventory against recently closed comps. By the time they walk into a showing they've formed a view on value — a home that confirms it moves; a home that challenges it doesn't. What's changed is the speed of the feedback loop. The market gives sellers a clear signal within two to three weeks: multiple inquiries and offers, or silence.

Every week a home sits, it becomes harder to explain to the next buyer who walks in. The market is patient. Sellers don't have to be — but pricing is the lever.

Transfer-tax changes: what sellers above $5.4M need to know before July 1.

LA's Measure ULA — the "mansion tax" passed in 2022 — has been a material consideration for upper-tier sellers since it took effect in 2023. As of July 1, 2026, the thresholds adjust: the 4% tier applies to sales above $5,400,000 (up from $5,150,000), and the 5.5% tier to sales above $10,900,000 (up from $10,300,000). These are City of LA taxes applied to the full sale price — on a $6M sale that's $240,000; on a $12M sale at 5.5%, $660,000.

Measure ULA transfer-tax thresholds — before vs. after July 1, 2026

City of Los Angeles mansion-tax tiers. Tax applies to the full sale price above each threshold.

The threshold increases are modest — they move the line but don't eliminate the tax. The more important consideration is the relationship between your asking price and the nearest threshold. A seller who prices at $5.5M writes a $220,000 check to the city on closing day; a seller at $5.3M avoids the tax entirely. Whether that price difference is supportable by the comps is a separate question — but one worth asking before you list.

Off-market activity: where the real deals are happening.

One of the more notable shifts in the past 30 days is the increase in off-market conversations. More sellers are testing the waters privately before committing to a public launch — quietly gauging interest and preserving optionality. What's changed is the volume and breadth across price tiers: not just above $3M, where off-market has long been common, but increasingly in the $1.2M–$2.5M range. For buyers, staying closely connected to an active agent is how you access inventory that never appears on Zillow or Redfin — see our Private Exclusives explainer. For sellers, the tradeoff is control versus exposure; whether it makes sense depends heavily on the property.

Key takeaways

  • Pricing accuracy is the primary driver of outcome — the gap between right-priced and aspirational has widened in both speed and net proceeds.
  • July 1 ULA thresholds adjust: 4% tier to $5.4M, 5.5% tier to $10.9M. Sellers near a threshold should model the impact.
  • Off-market activity is rising across price tiers — buyers need close agent relationships to access it.
  • The most important deals are increasingly happening behind the scenes.
