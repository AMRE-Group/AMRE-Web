Modernism Week: the desert's architectural main event.

A celebration of mid-century architecture, design culture, and the enduring real estate appeal of Palm Springs — as a getaway, a design pilgrimage, and a portfolio play.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · April 14, 2026 · 4 min read

Every year, Palm Springs Modernism Week turns the desert into the world's largest open-air museum of mid-century design — home tours, expert-led bus tours, lectures, film screenings, and exhibitions celebrating the architects who defined desert modernism. As an architect-turned-agent, it's the one event I recommend to every client who cares about design. Here are the icons worth planning a trip around, and why this festival keeps mattering to the real estate market beneath it.

Kaufmann Desert House: Neutra's masterpiece.

The Kaufmann Desert House, designed by Richard Neutra in 1946, is the quintessential desert modernist residence. Clean lines, expansive glass walls, and a plan that dissolves into the surrounding landscape — the interplay of light, space, and nature still feels contemporary nearly eighty years on. A tour offers an intimate look at why Neutra's influence shows up in half the homes built in the hills above Los Angeles today, including the named-architect homes we track for clients.

The Elvis Honeymoon Hideaway: glamour and history.

The Elvis Presley Honeymoon Hideaway is as much cultural landmark as architectural wonder. The house where Elvis and Priscilla spent their honeymoon features bold geometric forms, curved rooflines, and palm-fringed courtyards that capture the forward-looking luxury of the 1960s — a fascinating stop for fans of design and pop history alike.

Dinah Shore Residence: a Wexler gem.

Donald Wexler's Dinah Shore Residence is mid-century elegance at full scale: open-plan living, floor-to-ceiling glass flooding the interior with light, and indoor-outdoor spaces that blur into the desert. Wexler's innovative use of steel and glass made him a legend of desert modernism, and this residence remains one of his most complete statements.

Why it matters for the market.

Palm Springs holds one of the densest concentrations of intact mid-century homes anywhere, and Modernism Week is the engine that renews global demand for them every single year. Architectural pedigree commands a real premium in the desert — the same dynamic we've documented for architect-designed homes in Los Angeles. For LA buyers, the desert is the natural second-home market: under two hours away, with strong seasonal rental demand and — as we covered in our Palm Springs summer guide — genuine off-season negotiating leverage for buyers in the $700K–$1M band.

Key takeaways

  • Modernism Week is the world's biggest celebration of mid-century design — flagship festival each February, fall preview in October.

  • Bus tours, lectures, films, and exhibitions make it approachable even without tour tickets to the marquee homes.
  • Design tourism sustains a durable premium on the desert's architectural housing stock.
  • For LA buyers, Palm Springs is the natural second-home play — especially in the off-season.

Frequently asked questions.

What is Palm Springs Modernism Week?

An annual festival of mid-century architecture and design built around home tours, bus tours, lectures, and exhibitions — flagship event each February, with a smaller October preview.

Which homes are the must-sees?

The Kaufmann Desert House (Neutra, 1946), the Elvis Presley Honeymoon Hideaway, and Donald Wexler's Dinah Shore Residence.

Is Palm Springs a good second-home market for LA buyers?

For design-minded Angelenos, yes — under two hours away, architecturally unique, with seasonal rental demand and off-season buyer leverage.
