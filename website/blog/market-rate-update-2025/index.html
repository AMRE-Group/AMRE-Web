Rates stayed high. Deals kept moving. Here's how.

The Fed cut, mortgage rates shrugged, and the forecast said "above 6% all year." What kept the Los Angeles market active was creative financing — buydowns, interest-only structures, and longer terms that make elevated rates workable.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · March 28, 2026 · 4 min read

The Federal Reserve cut its benchmark rate by 25 basis points to 4.5%–4.75%, lowering Prime to 7.75% — genuinely good news for anyone carrying a HELOC. But mortgage rates didn't see an immediate dip, because the bond market had already priced the move in. Bright MLS Chief Economist Lisa Sturtevant's forecast captured the consensus: continued volatility, with rates likely staying above 6% throughout 2025. That forecast largely held — and it taught buyers and sellers a durable lesson about how deals get done when cheap money doesn't come back.

Why the Fed cut didn't move mortgages.

Mortgage rates track long-term bond yields and expectations, not the Fed's overnight rate. When a cut is fully anticipated, it's already in the price of your quote before the announcement. That's why the practical playbook isn't "wait for the Fed" — it's structuring around the rate you can get today, with optionality if rates fall later.

The creative financing playbook.

Higher rates are a hurdle, not a wall. These are the three structures we saw put to work most effectively:

01

2-1 buydown

Seller funds a temporary rate cut: 2 points lower in year one, 1 point in year two. Often cheaper for the seller than a price reduction — and more valuable to the buyer.

02

Interest-only loan

Lower payments up front, with a plan to refinance when rates drop. Suits rising incomes and investors prioritizing early cash flow.

03

40-year term

Smaller monthly payments spread over a longer horizon. Flexibility now, traded against slower equity build.

Each is a tool, not a default. The right structure depends on your hold period, income trajectory, and risk tolerance — which is exactly the conversation to have before you shop, whether you're buying or fielding offers as a seller deciding between a buydown credit and a price cut. What it means for strategy.

When rates stay elevated, the market rewards preparation over timing. Buyers who structure around today's rate — and refinance if tomorrow's is better — avoid competing with the crowd that floods back in when rates finally drop. Sellers who offer financing concessions often net more than those who simply cut price. Key takeaways

  • Fed cuts don't automatically lower mortgage rates — the bond market prices moves in ahead of time.
  • Rates above 6% became the planning baseline, not a temporary shock.
  • 2-1 buydowns let sellers buy demand more efficiently than price cuts.
  • Interest-only and 40-year structures trade equity build for payment flexibility — know the trade before signing.

Frequently asked questions.

Why didn't mortgage rates drop when the Fed cut?

Mortgages track bond-market expectations, not the overnight rate. An anticipated cut is already priced in — though Prime did fall, helping HELOC borrowers immediately.

What is a 2-1 buydown?

A seller-funded temporary rate reduction — 2 points lower in year one, 1 in year two — that eases buyers into the payment, often at lower cost to the seller than a price cut.

Should I wait for lower rates to buy?

Waiting has a cost: when rates fall meaningfully, demand and prices typically rise together. Many buyers secure today's price and refinance later instead.
