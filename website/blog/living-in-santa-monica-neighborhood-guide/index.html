Living in Santa Monica.

Santa Monica is three distinct residential markets, one of the best dining cities on the West Coast, and three and a half miles of Pacific shoreline. Here is what buying and living here actually looks like in 2026.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · May 7, 2026 · 7 min read

Santa Monica is the most expensive city-within-a-city on the Los Angeles Westside, and for buyers who understand why, the premium is not hard to justify. It sits at the terminus of the 10 Freeway and the Metro E Line. It has 3.5 miles of Pacific beachfront. It has an independent school district that outperforms the surrounding system. And it has a level of genuine, everyday walkability that almost no other part of Los Angeles can offer.

But Santa Monica is not one market. It is three, stacked on top of each other geographically — and they price accordingly.

North of Montana

The streets north of Montana Avenue, running up to San Vicente Boulevard and toward Santa Monica Canyon, are among the most consistently desirable single-family blocks in Southern California. Entry starts around $3.5–5 million for a well-maintained but unremarkable home, moving into the $6–12 million range for larger properties and meaningful renovations. The buyer profile skews toward families with school-age children — SMMUSD's elementary pipeline here significantly reduces private-school dependency, and that school premium is real and measurable.

Mid-City Santa Monica and the Wilshire corridor

The mid-city section — south of Montana, north of Pico, between Lincoln and Ocean — is the most varied of the three. It spans condominiums along the Wilshire high-rise corridor, single-family homes in established pockets, and the mixed-use activity around Third Street and Main Street. The range is broad: from $800,000 for a well-positioned condo to $3 million for a larger single-family home. Bergamot Station — a converted 1875 rail depot with multiple galleries — anchors genuine cultural density within walking distance of residential blocks.

Ocean Park and Sunset Park

South of Pico, Ocean Park and Sunset Park are more residential, more neighborhood-scaled, and more affordable relative to the rest of the city — single-family homes run roughly $1.4–2.8 million. Proximity to Venice adds a cultural overlay, and beach access is equivalent to any other part of the city. For buyers who want SMMUSD schools and Santa Monica beach access at a lower entry point, this is the sub-market to focus on — we covered Sunset Park in its own guide, and for coastal budgets below these ranges, see beach properties under $1 million.

The dining scene.

Santa Monica's restaurant scene is exceptional by any standard. Mélisse — Josiah Citrin's Michelin-starred dining room on Wilshire — has defined LA fine dining for over twenty years. Cassia, Bryant Ng's Southeast Asian brasserie on Broadway, has held a place on every serious best-of list since it opened. Bay Cities Italian Deli on Lincoln has a near-mythological reputation — the Godmother sandwich still generates lines decades on. Rustic Canyon is the neighborhood's most beloved casual fine dining room, and Gjusta in adjacent Venice is worth any wait at any hour.

The beach, the pier, and the public realm.

Santa Monica's public realm is among the best in Southern California, full stop. Palisades Park — 26 acres of clifftop gardens above PCH — offers unobstructed Pacific views from Malibu to Palos Verdes. Tongva Park and Ken Genser Square, designed by the team behind New York's High Line, are award-winning landscape architecture open to everyone. The Santa Monica Pier, historic since 1909 and the western terminus of Route 66, is one of California's most recognizable landmarks — and for residents, simply a place to walk on a Sunday morning. The Marvin Braude Bike Trail — The Strand — runs 22 miles of coastal path from Pacific Palisades to Palos Verdes; for buyers who cycle, it's commuting infrastructure, not just recreation.

What buyers need to know.

Santa Monica has strict rent control (the 1979 Rent Control Charter Amendment applies to most pre-1979 rental housing), strong tenant protections, and significant short-term rental restrictions. For income-property buyers, understanding the regulatory environment is essential before committing. The planning process is notably active, and entitlement timelines should be modeled conservatively.

The off-market is meaningful north of Montana: homes there rarely need to advertise, and brokers often match transactions without a public listing. Being connected to the right network here has direct financial value — something we help buyers with directly.

Key takeaways

  • Three markets: North of Montana ($3.5–12M), mid-city ($800K condos to $3M homes), Ocean Park / Sunset Park ($1.4–2.8M).
  • Schools: SMMUSD is one of California's top districts — the primary driver of single-family demand.
  • Investors: strict 1979 rent control applies to most pre-1979 rentals — model it before buying.
  • Lifestyle: 3.5 miles of beach, Palisades Park, the Pier, The Strand, and true everyday walkability.
  • Transit: Metro E Line western terminus — direct rail to Culver City, USC, and Downtown.

Frequently asked questions.

Is Santa Monica a good place to live?

Widely considered one of the best urban living environments in Southern California: beachfront, top public schools, award-winning parks, world-class dining, and rail access to the rest of LA.

What are the best neighborhoods in Santa Monica?

North of Montana for prestige and schools; Mid-City Santa Monica for the Wilshire corridor and condo entry points; Ocean Park and Sunset Park for value, character, and proximity to Venice.

What is the average home price in Santa Monica?

North of Montana starts around $3.5–5M and reaches $6–12M; mid-city condos start near $800K; Ocean Park and Sunset Park homes run about $1.4–2.8M. Santa Monica is consistently among the five most expensive markets in LA County.

Does Santa Monica have rent control?

Yes — most rental housing built before 1979 falls under the city's rent control ordinance, with strict caps on increases and strong eviction protections.

How far is Santa Monica from Downtown LA?

About 15 miles — 25–45 minutes by car, or 45–50 minutes on the Metro E Line with no transfers.
