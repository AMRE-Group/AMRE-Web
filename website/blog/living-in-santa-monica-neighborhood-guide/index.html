Living in Santa Monica.

Santa Monica is three distinct residential markets, one of the best dining cities on the West Coast, and three and a half miles of Pacific shoreline. Here is what buying and living here actually looks like in 2026.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · May 7, 2026 · 7 min read

Santa Monica is the most expensive city-within-a-city on the Los Angeles Westside, and for buyers who understand why, the premium is not hard to justify. It sits at the terminus of the 10 Freeway and the Metro E Line. It has 3.5 miles of Pacific beachfront. It has an independent school district that outperforms the surrounding system. And it has a level of walkability — genuine, pleasant, everyday walkability — that almost no other part of Los Angeles can offer.

North of Montana.

The streets north of Montana Avenue, running up to San Vicente Boulevard and toward Santa Monica Canyon, are among the most consistently desirable single-family blocks in Southern California — large lots, mature tree canopy, walking distance to Montana Avenue's shopping and dining, and easy access to Pacific Palisades and Malibu. Entry starts around $3.5–5 million for a well-maintained home on a standard lot and moves into the $6–12 million range for larger, renovated properties. The buyer profile skews toward families: SMMUSD's elementary schools feed a pipeline that meaningfully reduces private-school dependency, and the school premium here is real and measurable.

Mid-City and the Wilshire corridor.

The mid-city section — south of Montana, north of Pico, between Lincoln and Ocean — is the most varied sub-market. It spans condominiums along the Wilshire high-rise corridor, single-family homes in established residential pockets, and the mixed-use activity around Third Street and Main Street. Prices run broad, from about $800,000 for a well-positioned condo to $3 million for a larger home on the best blocks. Bergamot Station Arts Center — a converted 1875 rail depot with multiple galleries — anchors a genuine cultural community here.

Ocean Park and Sunset Park.

South of Pico, Ocean Park and Sunset Park feel more residential and neighborhood-scaled — and more affordable relative to the rest of the city. Single-family homes run roughly $1.4–2.8 million. Proximity to Venice adds a cultural overlay, and beach access is equivalent to anywhere else in Santa Monica. For buyers who want the Santa Monica school district and beach at a lower entry point than the north side, this is the sub-market to focus on — see our deep dive on Sunset Park.

The dining scene.

Santa Monica's restaurant scene is exceptional by any standard. Mélisse — Josiah Citrin's Michelin-starred room on Wilshire — has been a definitive LA fine-dining experience for over twenty years. Cassia on Broadway, Bryant Ng's Southeast Asian brasserie, has held its place on every serious best-of list since it opened. Bay Cities Italian Deli on Lincoln has a near-mythological reputation for the Godmother sandwich, and Rustic Canyon Wine Bar is the neighborhood's most beloved casual fine-dining room.

The beach, the pier, and the public realm.

Santa Monica's public realm is among the best in Southern California, full stop. Palisades Park — 26 acres of clifftop gardens along Ocean Avenue — offers unobstructed Pacific views from Malibu to Palos Verdes. Tongva Park and Ken Genser Square, designed by James Corner Field Operations (the team behind New York's High Line), are award-winning landscape architecture. And the 22-mile Marvin Braude Bike Trail — The Strand — begins right here.

What buyers need to know.

The single most important non-obvious factor for investors is rent control: Santa Monica's 1979 ordinance applies to most pre-1979 rental housing, with strict caps on increases and strong eviction protections. For owner-occupant buyers, the decision usually comes down to school priority and budget — North of Montana for the schools-and-lot premium, Ocean/Sunset Park for the same district at a lower entry, or the Wilshire corridor for walkable condo living.

Key takeaways

  • Three markets, not one: North of Montana ($3.5–12M), Mid-City/Wilshire ($800K condos–$3M), Ocean/Sunset Park ($1.4–2.8M).
  • SMMUSD is one of California's top public districts — the core of the North-of-Montana premium.
  • The 1979 rent-control ordinance is decisive for investors evaluating pre-1979 buildings.
  • Ocean Park and Sunset Park offer the same schools and beach access at the lowest entry point.
  • 3.5 miles of beachfront, award-winning parks, and the Metro E Line to Downtown without a car.
