Running the Numbers on a Value-Add Deal.

A step-by-step framework for analyzing whether a fixer-upper actually pencils out — before you fall in love with the potential and overlook the math.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · April 25, 2026 · 6 min read

Few investment vehicles offer the stability, control, and wealth-building potential of real estate in an uncertain economy. Executed well, a value-add flip can generate significant short-term returns while laying groundwork for long-term financial freedom. But "potential" is not a number — and the fastest way to lose money is to fall in love with a property before you underwrite it.

So let's run a real one. 2932 Farmdale Ave is a 3-bed, 2-bath single-family home — 1,955 sq ft on a 5,851 sq ft lot — in the rapidly appreciating West Adams / Mid-City corner of Los Angeles. It traded at $899,303 (about $460/SF), well below the $750–$1,000/SF that renovated comps command. That spread is the whole opportunity. Here is how we pressure-test it.

The all-in cost, line by line.

Where the deal pencils.

Renovated comps support $750–$1,000/SF. Underwrite conservatively at a $825/SF target exit, and this 1,955 sq ft home resells around $1,611,375. Subtract 5% selling costs and your all-in basis, and the profit is real — not aspirational.

The metrics that matter.

  • ROI range: ~10% to 25%, depending on where the market lets you exit.
  • 6-month IRR: ~21% to ~49% — annualized, because timeline is everything on a flip.
  • Breakeven PPSF: ~$670/SF. Your target of $825/SF is the cushion.
  • Alternative exit: hold as a rental at $5,000–$6,000/mo for a 5–6% cap rate, with ADU and value-add upside layered on top.
Potential is a story. A pro forma is a decision. Never confuse the two.

What makes a deal pencil

  • All-in basis (purchase + reno + closing, holding, financing) sits well below comp-supported resale.
  • The exit price you underwrite is conservative — real comps, not the top of the range.
  • Breakeven PPSF leaves a visible margin of safety below your target exit.
  • You've budgeted a 10% contingency and a realistic 6-month timeline.
  • The fallback exit — hold, rent, or 1031 into the next deal — still works if the flip market softens.

This is the same underwriting we run for every buyer and investor we work with: cap rate, cash-on-cash, ROI, and IRR on real comps — architect-informed diligence on the renovation scope so the budget is grounded, not guessed.
