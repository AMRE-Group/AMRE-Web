Venice Condos for Sale:
2026 Buyer's Guide

By Michael Abraham · May 29, 2026 · 7 min read

Venice condo inventory is unlike anything else on the Westside — beach adjacency, canal frontage, architect-designed lofts, and a cultural identity that commands a persistent premium. Here is what buyers actually need to know before making an offer.

The Venice condo market in 2026

Venice remains one of the most liquid condo markets on the LA Westside. Inventory turns fast, particularly within four blocks of the beach and on the canals. Buyers who understand the sub-markets — and the HOA and rent control nuances that can make or break a deal — consistently win over those who don't.

The four Venice condo sub-markets

1. Canal-adjacent

The Venice Canals — Carroll, Eastern, Sherman, Linnie, and Howland — are the single most desirable condo location in Venice. Inventory is extremely limited; canal-fronting condos almost never come to market and frequently trade above ask. From an architectural standpoint, the canal corridor contains some of the most thoughtfully designed small-lot housing in LA. Budget $1.8M–$3M+ for anything with actual canal frontage.

2. Abbot Kinney corridor

The blocks flanking Abbot Kinney Boulevard attract design-forward buyers — newer loft-style condos with exposed concrete, rooftop decks, and architect pedigrees. This is where the "creative premium" is most pronounced. HOA fees tend to be higher (reflecting better amenities), and units move quickly. Typical range: $1.2M–$2.5M for 2BR+.

3. Beach-adjacent (Speedway / Breeze Ave)

The blocks between the boardwalk and Lincoln draw buyers who want ocean proximity above all else. Product is mixed — older converted buildings alongside newer construction. Walk scores are among the highest in LA. HOA reserves are the biggest due-diligence risk here; many older buildings are significantly underfunded. Range: $800K–$1.5M for 1-2BR.

4. Inland Venice (Lincoln / Venice Blvd)

More affordable entry into the Venice ZIP code. Access to the neighborhood without beach premiums. Increasingly popular with buyers priced out of the canal district. Range: $700K–$1.2M. Condo product here is more conventional — this is where a buyer focused on investment yield rather than lifestyle premium will find the best numbers.

Architect's note: Venice has more award-winning small-lot and condo architecture per block than anywhere else in LA — but quality varies enormously. Before any offer, I walk every unit with a contractor's eye: structural modifications, non-permitted additions, and deferred maintenance in older buildings are common. That walkthrough is free for AMRE clients.

HOA due diligence: the #1 issue buyers miss

Venice condo HOA issues are more common than in most LA markets because of the building age mix. Before you remove contingencies, get:

  • The reserve study — look for funded ratio below 50%; anything under 30% is a red flag
  • Minutes from the last 24 months of HOA board meetings — litigation, special assessments, deferred maintenance discussions
  • The last 3 years of financial statements — operating budget vs actuals
  • Any pending or threatened legal action against or by the HOA
  • Insurance certificates — earthquake coverage is frequently absent in older buildings

A special assessment — where the HOA levies all owners for a large repair — can run $20K–$80K per unit in older Venice buildings. This is not theoretical; it happens regularly in buildings with deferred maintenance and thin reserves.

Rent control: what Venice condo buyers need to know

This is the question we get most often. The short answer: most owner-occupied condos in Venice are not subject to LA's Rent Stabilization Ordinance (RSO), but there are exceptions that matter.

The key test is whether the building was a rental property as of October 1, 1978 (the RSO effective date). Buildings constructed after that date are generally exempt. Buildings that were multi-unit rentals before that date — even if subsequently converted to condos — may have RSO-protected tenants in certain units. We verify this on every transaction before close.

Short-term rentals and Airbnb

LA's Home Sharing ordinance limits short-term rentals (under 30 days) to the host's primary residence only, requires a permit, and caps rental nights. Venice condos cannot legally operate as investment Airbnb properties by an absentee owner. Many buyers assume otherwise — confirm the HOA's rental rules and LA city compliance before buying for STR income.

Venice condo FAQs

What is the average price of a condo in Venice, CA?

Venice condo prices in 2026 range from approximately $800K–$1.1M for a 1-bedroom within a few blocks of the beach, to $1.5M–$2.5M for larger 2-3 bedroom units with rooftop decks, design finishes, or canal-adjacent positions. Architect-designed or award-winning buildings command a further premium. The Abbot Kinney corridor and canals add roughly 15–25% over comparable inland product.

Are Venice condos subject to rent control?

Condos in Venice are generally NOT subject to LA rent control (RSO) if they are single-ownership units in a post-1978 condominium structure — but if the building was previously a multi-unit rental converted to condos before the RSO date, specific units may retain RSO protection. Always verify the rent control status of any specific unit before making an offer.

What are typical HOA fees for Venice condos?

Venice condo HOA fees range from $300–$500/month for older buildings with minimal amenities, to $800–$1,500/month for newer buildings with rooftop decks, concierge, or underground parking. Always review the HOA reserve study — underfunded reserves are common in older Venice buildings and can signal a special assessment within 2–3 years.

Which streets are best for buying a condo in Venice?

The most sought-after condo locations in Venice are: the Venice Canals (Carroll, Eastern, Sherman, Linnie — rare, very low turnover), the Abbot Kinney corridor (design-forward lofts), and blocks between Venice Blvd and the beach. Speedway and boardwalk-adjacent streets have the most foot traffic and rental demand but more transient energy.

Can I rent out a Venice condo on Airbnb?

Short-term rentals (under 30 days) in LA require a Home Sharing Permit and are limited to your primary residence. Venice condos can typically be rented long-term (30+ days) subject to HOA rules — review the CC&Rs carefully, as many newer HOAs restrict or prohibit all rentals.
