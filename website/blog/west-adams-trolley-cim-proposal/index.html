An open proposal to CIM Group — build the West Adams trolley.

The West Adams corridor is going to break under its own success unless someone solves two problems at once: there's no efficient way to move people across it, and nowhere to put their cars. Both have one elegant solution — and for CIM, it's not charity. It's the highest-return capital on the corridor.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · May 4, 2026 · 7 min read

This is a follow-up to our earlier piece on what's happening on the West Adams corridor — the buildings, the restaurants, the Museum of Ice Cream flagship. That one described the transformation without an agenda. This one has one: the corridor needs a way to move people and a place to put cars, and the developer most exposed to the downside is the one best positioned to fund the fix.

The problem CIM just built for itself.

By the time the filed and under-construction projects deliver, the Adams Boulevard corridor between Crenshaw and La Cienega will host roughly 550 new apartments, 85,000+ sq ft of retail, a 48-unit short-term-stay building, and the 23,000-sq-ft Museum of Ice Cream flagship — which, based on the operator's other locations, could draw upwards of 700,000 visitors a year. All concentrated on a 3.3-mile stretch that the Metro Expo Line technically serves but practically does not (its stations sit two long blocks south, on Exposition).

Then there's parking. New residential buildings are entitled with one space per unit at most — 0.5 in some cases — and retail carries minimal off-street parking under the city's Transit Oriented Communities rules. Add it up and the peak looks stark.

There is no public parking structure between Crenshaw and La Cienega — the estimated deficit on a peak Saturday is about 1,070 spaces. That gap doesn't show up tomorrow; it shows up the day the Museum of Ice Cream opens. And the corridor's response, on its current trajectory, will be the usual one: ad-hoc valet on the sidewalks, residents calling enforcement, restaurant guests circling and leaving, and a slow erosion of the foot-traffic environment that made the corridor desirable in the first place.

The demand for a fix isn't theoretical — it's already on CIM's balance sheet. As of this writing, CIM's leasing portal lists at least nine available retail and office spaces on the corridor, a combined ~28,000 sq ft of unleased commercial inventory (roughly a third of its listed retail). Operators don't sign leases on a corridor where customers can't reasonably get to the door. The trolley is also the leasing strategy.

The plan.

A free, electric, branded trolley running Crenshaw to La Cienega, both directions, every 12–15 minutes. The route covers roughly 3.3 miles with about 12 stops at the corridor's anchors — every CIM building with ground-floor retail, every major restaurant, the museum entrance, and, critically, a central parking hub. Two vehicles in service give a wait of about seven minutes at any stop.

The proposed route, at a glance

  • 3.3 miles end-to-end · about a 25–30 minute full loop.
  • ~12 stops at retail, restaurants, and the museum.
  • ~7-minute wait with two vehicles running.
  • A 300-space hub embedded in the proposed 5451 W. Adams project.

The hub is the key. The proposed 5451 W. Adams project sits near the corridor's geographic and economic center — and it's the one CIM project still in entitlement, so its parking program isn't finalized. Build the structure now, while the design is on the drafting table, and the marginal cost is a fraction of a standalone deck. Park at 5451, take the trolley either direction, spend the day, come back when you're ready to leave.

This is what LA used to be.

The strongest endorsement for this proposal isn't a modern urban-design study — it's the actual history of Los Angeles. Developer-funded transit didn't just exist here; it built the city.

1901

Pacific Electric Railway

Henry Huntington's red cars reached 1,100+ miles — the world's largest electric railway, funded by real estate to move buyers to the lots for sale.

1901

The Balloon Route

Sherman & Clark's developer-owned trolley tour from Downtown to the beach — openly a rolling open house that turned riders into homeowners.

2003

The WeHo PickUp

A free six-mile shuttle on Santa Monica Blvd, funded by the city to drive retail foot traffic. Still running, still free, still working.

The Adams Boulevard streetcar — the real one, Pacific Electric line 6 — ran exactly this corridor. It's a measure of how thoroughly LA forgot itself postwar that the simplest infrastructure move a major corridor could make in 2026 is to put back a smaller, electric, free version of what used to be there. The point isn't nostalgia; it's that private capital paying for fixed-route transit because it lifts the surrounding real estate is exactly what built this city.

The cost — and why it's smaller than you think.

Two electric vehicles running roughly 60 hours a week (say 11am–11pm, Thursday–Sunday) is a known cost structure: about $800K–$1.2M annually, in the same band as Long Beach's LB Circuit, the WeHo PickUp, and Glendale's Beeline. Now compare that to the asset base it serves.

The breakeven rent lift across the full portfolio is under 0.8%. A boutique, branded, named-after-the-corridor trolley becomes a marketing asset — every leasing brochure can say "complimentary trolley to all corridor amenities." A 1% lift covers the cost; a 5% lift — defensible where walkability sustains real premiums — makes it one of the best NOI investments in the company's portfolio. And that's before the harder-to-measure wins: parking pressure lifted off residential streets, foot traffic delivered to tenants, and the gravitational pull of the one mid-city neighborhood where you don't think about parking.

The trolley pays for itself in NOI before it ever gets to the part where it pays for itself in goodwill.

The bigger argument.

There's a moment in every neighborhood transformation where success or stall comes down to whether someone solves the friction problems before they turn political. West Adams is in that moment now. The pipeline is real, the cultural anchors are in place, and the museum opens in 2026. The next thing that happens, if nothing changes, is that the corridor's first busy weekends become its first negative news cycle — about parking, traffic, and gentrification.

The trolley dissolves that. It moves people instead of cars, fills retail seats, and shifts the story from "developer is changing the neighborhood" to "developer is investing in its mobility." The hub site is already on the drafting table. The historical model is a hundred years old and was invented in this exact city. It's time to build the thing.

FAQ

The West Adams trolley, answered.

What is being proposed for the West Adams corridor?

A free, electric, branded trolley running the length of Adams Boulevard from Crenshaw to La Cienega — roughly 3.3 miles, about 12 stops, every 12–15 minutes — anchored by a central 300-space parking hub built into the proposed 5451 W. Adams project. Park once, ride the corridor, and never move your car.

Why is there a parking problem in West Adams?

The corridor's built and entitled pipeline — roughly 550 apartments, 85,000+ sq ft of retail, a 48-unit short-term-stay building, and the Museum of Ice Cream flagship — creates an estimated Saturday-peak demand of about 1,790 spaces against only ~720 on-corridor spaces. There is no public parking structure between Crenshaw and La Cienega, leaving a deficit of roughly 1,070 spaces.

How much would the West Adams trolley cost?

A two-vehicle electric trolley running about 60 hours a week costs roughly $800,000–$1.2 million a year to operate, in line with the WeHo PickUp, Long Beach's LB Circuit, and Glendale's Beeline. Against CIM's ~550-unit, ~85,000-sq-ft portfolio, a rent premium under 1% covers the cost.

Has developer-funded transit worked in LA before?

Yes — it built the city. The Pacific Electric Railway, the world's largest electric railway at its 1920s peak, was funded largely by land developers to move buyers to the lots they were selling. The original Adams Boulevard streetcar was part of that network. The free WeHo PickUp shuttle has done the same for retail foot traffic since 2003.

Where is West Adams and what's happening there?

West Adams is a historic mid-city Los Angeles corridor along Adams Boulevard, roughly between Crenshaw and La Cienega. It's in the middle of a major transformation — new apartments, ground-floor retail, restaurants, and the Museum of Ice Cream flagship — driving strong buyer and investor interest in the surrounding neighborhood.
