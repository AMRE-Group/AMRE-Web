An Open Proposal to CIM Group: Build the West Adams Trolley.

West Adams's biggest constraint isn't capital — CIM has supplied that. It's how people move across a corridor about to host fifty buildings, hundreds of apartments, a Michelin-caliber restaurant scene, and a Museum of Ice Cream flagship. The fix is older than the freeway: a free trolley, anchored by a parking hub at the new 5451 W. Adams project.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · May 4, 2026 · 8 min read

This is a follow-up to last week's piece on what's actually happening on the West Adams corridor. That first article described the transformation without an editorial agenda. This one has one.

The corridor is going to break under its own success unless someone solves two problems at once: there's no efficient way to move people across it, and there's nowhere to put their cars. Both problems have a single, elegant solution — and once you look at the math, funding it isn't a charitable act. It's the highest-return capital deployment available to CIM Group on the corridor.

The problem CIM just built for itself.

By the time the projects already filed or under construction are delivered, the Adams Boulevard corridor between Crenshaw and La Cienega will host roughly 550 new apartment units, 85,000+ square feet of ground-floor retail, a 48-unit short-term-stay building, and the 23,000-square-foot Museum of Ice Cream flagship — a destination likely to draw upwards of 700,000 visitors a year. All concentrated along a 3.3-mile stretch that the Metro Expo Line technically serves but practically does not.

That deficit doesn't show up tomorrow — it shows up the day the museum opens. And the corridor's default response would be the same one every LA neighborhood has to that problem: ad-hoc valet on the sidewalks, residents calling parking enforcement, guests circling for twenty minutes and leaving, and a slow erosion of the very foot-traffic environment that made the corridor desirable.

The demand is already on CIM's balance sheet.

This isn't theoretical. As of this writing, CIM Group's leasing portal lists at least nine actively available retail and office spaces on the corridor — a combined ~28,000 square feet of unleased commercial inventory targeting "restaurant, service, entertainment, grocery, and fitness" tenants. The category that signs those leases is precisely the one that requires reliable, walkable foot traffic.

Every empty storefront is forgone NOI. The trolley directly addresses why those spaces aren't leasing — operators don't sign on a corridor where customers can't easily reach the door. The trolley is the leasing strategy.

The plan.

A free, electric, branded trolley runs the length of the corridor — Crenshaw to La Cienega, both directions, every twelve to fifteen minutes. The full 3.3-mile loop runs 25–30 minutes; two vehicles in service give a wait of about seven minutes at any stop. Roughly twelve stops sit at the corridor's anchors — every CIM building with ground-floor retail, every major restaurant, the museum entrance, and a central parking hub.

01

Park once at the hub

A ~300-space structure embedded in the 5451 W. Adams project — the one CIM site still in entitlement, so the parking program isn't finalized. Build it now and the marginal cost is a fraction of a standalone deck.

02

Ride either direction

Hop the trolley toward Crenshaw or La Cienega. First hour of parking free for everyone; validated for three hours through any participating restaurant or retailer.

03

Explore the corridor

Lunch at Maydan Market, the museum for the kids, dinner at Cento — without moving your car three times. Come back when you're ready to leave.

This is what LA used to be.

The strongest endorsement isn't a contemporary urban-design study — it's the actual history of Los Angeles. The Pacific Electric Railway, at its 1920s peak the largest electric railway in the world with 1,100+ miles of track, was built almost entirely by land developers. Henry Huntington, Moses Sherman, Eli Clark — they built streetcars because moving people across the parcels they owned was the most direct way to turn raw land into salable real estate.

The streetcar was the listing tour

The famous early-1900s "Balloon Route" from Downtown to the beach was, openly, a real-estate marketing engine — designed to deliver Sunday families past the lots that were for sale. The brochures didn't hide it. Developer-funded transit built modern LA; it can rebuild this corridor.

Why the math works for CIM.

At roughly $1.0M a year to run two vehicles ~60 hours a week, the breakeven is astonishingly low: across CIM's ~550 units and ~85K sq ft of retail, a rent lift under 0.8% covers the entire operating cost. Anything beyond that is profit — and a walkability amenity comfortably supports far more.

Capitalize a +$1.1M annual NOI improvement at a 5% cap rate and the implied portfolio value lift is $22M+ — before counting the harder-to-measure benefits: parking pressure kept off residential streets (which keeps neighbors from organizing against future entitlements), foot traffic delivered to tenants, and the gravitational pull of the only mid-city neighborhood where you don't think about parking.

What it looks like in practice.

The branding writes itself — call it The Adams Line, wrap the vehicles in a forest-green and brass palette that nods to the corridor's architecture, and brand it by the neighborhood, not by CIM. This is a public amenity CIM happens to fund, the way Anschutz funds LA Live or the Grove operates with private management. For an Instagram-native brand like the Museum of Ice Cream, "park free at 5451 and ride a trolley two stops to the entrance" becomes part of the experience itself.

West Adams is in the moment where the difference between a successful corridor and a stalled one comes down to whether someone solves the friction problems before they become political. The trolley moves people instead of cars, fills retail seats, and shifts the story from "developer is changing the neighborhood" to "developer is investing in its mobility." That's not spin — it's literally what would be happening.

Key takeaways

  • The built + entitled corridor faces a ~1,070-space Saturday parking deficit — with zero public garages today.
  • ~28,000 sq ft of CIM retail sits unleased; foot traffic is the missing ingredient, and the trolley supplies it.
  • A free 3.3-mile trolley plus a ~300-space hub at 5451 W. Adams lets visitors park once and explore the whole corridor.
  • Operating cost (~$1.0M/yr) is covered by a rent lift under 0.8%; a 5% lift implies $22M+ in portfolio value.
  • Developer-funded transit is a century-old LA tradition — the Pacific Electric was built to sell real estate.

If you're weighing a purchase or investment nearby, it's worth understanding the corridor's trajectory first — see the 4-unit West Adams pro forma and the wider West Adams neighborhood guide. The hub site is already on the drafting table. The historical model is a hundred years old and was invented in this exact city. It's time to build the thing.
