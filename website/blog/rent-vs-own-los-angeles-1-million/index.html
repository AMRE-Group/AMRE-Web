Rent vs. Own a $1M LA Home — The Honest Math.

We ran the real numbers on a million-dollar home against a comparable rental in West Adams, Culver City, and South LA. The result surprised us — and it points to the one move that quietly changes everything.

Here's a question we get almost every week: "Should I keep renting, or is it finally time to buy?" It usually comes with a knot of anxiety — the sense that renting is throwing money away, that prices only go up, that you're falling behind. So we did something most agents won't do. We built the real model, used today's rates, picked a real rental comp, and let the numbers land wherever they land — even when they didn't flatter the case for buying.

The honest answer is more interesting than "buy now." Let's walk through it together.

The setup: a $1M home vs. a real rental

A million dollars is a lot of money. It's also, frankly, the going rate for an entry-level home across much of LA right now. So that's our buyer's target: a $1,000,000 home — a 2-bed, 2-bath in the kind of neighborhood our clients actually shop: West Adams, Culver City, and parts of South LA. These pockets have great bones, real architecture, and momentum.

On the other side, our renter isn't comparing apples to mansions. They rent a comparable 2-bed, 2-bath in the same neighborhoods. As of May 2026, that runs about $3,200 a month — Culver City trends higher (~$3,400+), West Adams lower (~$2,400), so we split the difference to keep it fair.

Now the buyer's costs. We're not going to cheat by showing you just the mortgage. We're counting everything it actually takes to own and operate the home, at three different down payments, at today's roughly 6.5% 30-year fixed rate.

What It Actually Costs Per Month — Rent vs. Own a $1M Home

Owning a $1M home costs roughly 2.3× a comparable rental each month — even before you furnish it. That gap is the whole story. The question is what you do with it.

All-in owner cost = principal & interest + property tax (~1.25% effective) + insurance + 1%/yr maintenance + PMI where down payment is under 20%. Rate ~6.5% 30-yr fixed. Figures approximate, as of May 2026.

So owning costs about $7,200 a month at 20% down, climbing to roughly $8,800 at 5% down once you add PMI and a bigger loan. Against a $3,200 rent, the owner is spending an extra $4,000–$5,600 every single month on housing.

That's the uncomfortable part. Now here's where it gets interesting.

The fair fight: rent and invest the difference

The smartest objection to buying isn't "renting is cheaper." It's this: take the down payment and that monthly difference, and invest it in an index fund instead. The S&P 500 has returned about 8% a year over the long run. So what if our renter is disciplined — banks the down payment, invests every dollar they save versus owning, and lets it compound?

We modeled exactly that. Same cash out the door for both people. The owner's money goes into equity and home appreciation (we assumed a steady 6% a year). The renter's money goes into the market at 8%. Here's where they land after ten years, at 20% down:

Net Wealth After 10 Years — Own vs. Rent + Invest the Difference

On equal cash, the disciplined renter-investor edges out the homeowner by about $149,000 after ten years. At a price-to-rent ratio this high, the math genuinely favors renting + investing — on paper.

Owner equity is net of ~6% selling costs; home appreciates 6%/yr. Renter invests down payment + monthly savings at 8%/yr. Both deploy equal total cash. Illustrative; not a forecast. As of May 2026.

Read that again, because it's the part most real estate articles will never tell you: if our renter actually invests the difference, they come out ahead. We checked it at 5, 7, and 10 years and across all three down payments — renting + investing wins or ties in every single one, by anywhere from $85,000 to $205,000.

The honest part

Owning a home is not the only way to build wealth, and at today's prices it isn't even the mathematically optimal one on a spreadsheet. But a spreadsheet assumes you behave like a spreadsheet. Almost nobody invests the difference every month for ten years. A mortgage, on the other hand, forces you to — automatically, relentlessly, whether you feel like it or not.

Why owners still win in real life

So if the math leans toward renting, why do homeowners consistently end up wealthier in study after study? Three reasons the spreadsheet can't capture:

1. Forced savings beat good intentions. The renter's edge depends entirely on investing $4,000+ every month, for a decade, without ever skimming it for a vacation or a car. Real humans don't. A mortgage payment isn't optional — so the equity gets built whether or not you have the discipline. That single behavioral fact erases most of the renter's paper advantage.

2. Your payment is frozen. Rent isn't. The biggest chunk of your mortgage — principal and interest — never changes for 30 years. Rent climbs about 3% annually, forever. Watch what that does over a decade:

The Slow-Motion Crossover — Rising Rent vs. a Frozen Mortgage

Your $3,200 rent becomes ~$4,300 in ten years and keeps climbing. Your mortgage principal & interest stays locked near $5,057. Rent doesn't catch the full cost of owning for decades — but every year, the gap quietly closes in the owner's favor.

Rent grows 3%/yr; mortgage P&I fixed at ~6.5% on an $800k loan (20% down). Taxes, insurance & maintenance drift modestly with inflation. Illustrative. As of May 2026.

In year one, owning looks expensive. By year fifteen, the renter is writing a check that grows every year while the owner's core payment hasn't budged since the day they signed. Inflation is the renter's enemy and the borrower's quiet friend.

3. Leverage. This is the one Buffett talks about.

The magic of a fixed-rate mortgage

Warren Buffett has called a long, fixed-rate mortgage one of the best instruments in the world — and famously described the 30-year fixed as a remarkably attractive deal for the borrower. Here's why he's right.

When you put $200,000 into an index fund and it returns 8%, you made 8% on your money. Clean and simple. But when you put $200,000 down on a $1,000,000 home and it appreciates 6%, you didn't make 6%. The home gained $60,000 — and you controlled the whole thing with $200,000. That's a 30% return on your cash, from appreciation alone. Put less down, and the leverage multiplies:

The Leverage Effect — Year-One Return on Your Cash

A 6% rise on a $1M home is $60,000. Measured against your actual cash in the deal, that's a 30% to 120% first-year return on equity — because you borrowed the rest at a fixed rate the bank can never raise.

Cash-on-cash from appreciation only ($60k on $1M), before costs, financing, and taxes. Leverage amplifies losses as well as gains. Illustrative. As of May 2026.

And here's the Buffett kicker: it's a one-directional bet in your favor. If rates fall, you refinance. If rates rise, you keep your cheap fixed payment and laugh. Meanwhile inflation slowly shrinks the real value of the debt — you repay tomorrow's cheaper dollars on yesterday's loan. No margin call. No bank can demand the money back if the price dips. There is no other financial instrument an ordinary person can access on those terms.

That leverage is exactly why real estate builds so much wealth despite "losing" on the monthly spreadsheet. Just remember leverage cuts both ways — it magnifies a downturn too, which is why owning is a long game, not a flip.

The bonus move: buy a duplex, let a tenant pay your mortgage

Now for the part that genuinely flips the entire analysis. Everything above assumed you buy a home and live in all of it. But what if you buy a duplex, live in one unit, and rent the other?

This is called house hacking, and because the property is owner-occupied, you can often buy a 2-to-4-unit building with as little as 5% down — the same low-down-payment terms as a starter home. A realistic LA duplex runs a touch above our $1M line, so let's price it honestly at $1,100,000:

  • Your all-in monthly carry: about $9,800 (mortgage, taxes, insurance, maintenance, PMI on 5% down).
  • Your tenant pays you: roughly $2,600 for the second 2-bedroom unit.
  • Your effective housing cost: about $7,200 a month — while you own and control a $1.1M appreciating asset.

Look at what just happened. You're now living for roughly what it costs to own a single-family home with 20% down — but you only put $55,000 in, not $200,000. A stranger is covering more than a quarter of your payment. You get the full leverage and appreciation on the larger asset. And as rents rise 3% a year, your tenant's contribution grows while your mortgage stays frozen.

Where the math finally flips

This is the move that turns "renting wins on paper" into "owning wins in reality." When someone else is paying down your leveraged, appreciating asset, the down payment you'd have invested in an index fund is instead controlling five to twenty times its value — and throwing off rental income on top. It's the closest thing to a cheat code in personal finance, and it's hiding in plain sight all over West Adams and South LA.

So... rent or own?

Here's our genuinely honest take. If you are a disciplined investor who will truly bank and invest every dollar you save by renting, renting is a perfectly rational, even mathematically superior, path — and we'll never tell you otherwise. Owning a home is one road to wealth, not the only one.

But for most people, owning wins because of how humans actually behave: it forces savings, freezes your biggest expense while rents climb, and hands you the single best piece of leverage in finance. And if you're willing to house-hack a duplex, the financial case stops being close and starts being lopsided in your favor.

The wrong question is "rent or own?" in the abstract. The right question is "what's the smart move for my cash, my timeline, and my discipline?" That's the conversation we love having — with a real spreadsheet, not a sales pitch.

Frequently asked questions

Is it cheaper to rent or buy in Los Angeles right now?

Month to month, renting is cheaper in 2026 — a comparable 2-bed rental runs about $3,200 versus roughly $7,200 to own a $1M home with 20% down. The gap narrows over time as rent rises ~3% a year while your mortgage principal and interest stay fixed.

Does renting and investing the difference really beat buying?

On these numbers, narrowly yes — if you actually invest the difference every month at around 8%. The catch is behavioral: a mortgage forces savings, while a brokerage account relies on discipline almost nobody sustains for a decade.

Why is a mortgage considered such good leverage?

A fixed-rate mortgage lets you control a full-priced, appreciating asset with a fraction of the cash, at a rate the lender can never raise, while inflation erodes the real value of the debt. A 6% gain on a $1M home is a 30% return on a $200k down payment — or about 120% on 5% down.

What is house hacking and can I do it in LA?

House hacking means buying a 2-to-4-unit property, living in one unit, and renting the rest — often with as little as 5% down because it's owner-occupied. In LA, a ~$1.1M duplex can carry ~$9,800/month, but a tenant paying ~$2,600 drops your effective cost near $7,200 while you keep all the leverage and appreciation.
