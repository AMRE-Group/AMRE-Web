Rent vs. Own a $1M Los Angeles Home — the honest math.

We ran the real numbers on a million-dollar home against a comparable rental in West Adams, Culver City, and South LA. The result surprised us — and it points to the one move that quietly changes everything.

Principal Agent, Architect · Updated July 8, 2026 · 9 min read

Here's a question we get almost every week: "Should I keep renting, or is it finally time to buy?" It usually comes with a knot of anxiety — the sense that renting is throwing money away, that prices only go up, that you're falling behind. So we built the real model, with today's rates and a real rental comp, and let the numbers land wherever they land. The answer is clearer than you might expect.

Key takeaways

  • Monthly reality: a comparable 2BR/2BA rents for ~$3,500; owning the same $1M home at 20% down costs ~$7,223 all-in — roughly double.
  • The correct comparison: deploy the same $200k into the home or an index fund. The owner's leveraged equity leads the renter's portfolio by $215k at 5 years and $573k at 10.
  • Automatic savings: a mortgage forces equity every month; most renters never actually invest the difference.
  • Leverage multiplies returns: 6% on a $1M home is a 30% cash-on-cash gain on $200k down; an index fund at 8% returns 8%.
  • The duplex is the best play: 5% down ($50k), a tenant paying $2k/mo, and your $50k builds ~$878k in equity by year 10.

The setup: same $200,000. Two paths.

Our subject is a $1,000,000 home — a 2-bed, 2-bath in the neighborhoods our clients are actively shopping right now: West Adams, Culver City, and South LA. The only question is what to do with the same $200,000 in cash. The owner puts $200,000 down (20%) and pays carrying costs from income each month. The renter invests the same $200,000 in a broad index fund at ~8% annually and leaves it alone, paying rent from income. Both deploy the same capital on day one — that's the honest apples-to-apples comparison, because in the real world nobody banks the monthly "difference" and reinvests it; that money goes toward rent.

The SFR owner spends about $7,200 a month; the duplex owner nets $6,928 after the tenant's contribution; the renter pays $3,500. The gap is real — and it doesn't disappear. What changes the calculus is what both parties do with their capital and what happens to it over time.

What is PMI? Private Mortgage Insurance is a monthly premium lenders require when your down payment is under 20%. It protects the lender, not you. On a $950,000 loan with 5% down, PMI runs about $673/month — roughly $8,000/year — and drops off automatically once your balance falls below 80% of the original value, typically around year 7–10. It's the price of getting into a $1M asset with $50,000 instead of $200,000.

The correct comparison: same $200k, different destination.

Most rent-vs-own analyses assume the renter invests every dollar they save. That's not realistic — that money pays rent. The honest version: both parties start with $200,000 and deploy it on day one, then pay their housing costs from income. Here's where that $200,000 ends up under each path.

The owner wins at every horizon — and the gap widens sharply with time. By year 10 the owner has built about $1,005,188 in equity (net of selling costs) versus the renter's $431,785 portfolio. That's a $573,000 advantage from the same $200,000 starting point. The renter's fund grows well; the owner's equity more than doubles it. The reason is leverage.

Why the owner wins: the leverage no one else offers you.

When you invest $200,000 in an index fund at 8%, you made 8% on $200,000. But when you put $200,000 down on a $1,000,000 home and it appreciates 6%, the entire asset gained $60,000 — and you controlled it with just $200,000. That's a 30% return on your cash from appreciation alone, in year one. The bank funded the rest at a fixed rate it can never raise.

Warren Buffett has called the long fixed-rate mortgage one of the best financial instruments a household can access. The loan is fixed while inflation erodes the real value of the debt; if rates fall, you refinance; if they rise, you keep your cheap payment. The bank can't call the loan if the market dips — no margin call, no forced liquidation. No comparable instrument lets an ordinary person deploy this kind of leverage on a real asset with this degree of downside protection.

Where your equity actually comes from.

Every mortgage payment does two jobs: paying interest and paying down the balance. That second piece — principal reduction — is equity you build whether you think about it or not. On top of that, the home's value is rising. In year one you pay down nearly $9,000 in principal; by year 10 that's about $121,791, on top of roughly $791,000 in appreciation and your original $200,000 down payment — about $1,112,639 in gross equity from a single $200k start. And here's the behavioral truth most analyses skip: almost nobody actually invests the difference. A mortgage removes the choice — every payment automatically builds equity. For most people that forced discipline is worth more than the theoretical return on the difference.

The other quiet advantage: your payment is frozen. Rent isn't.

The biggest piece of your mortgage (principal and interest) is locked for 30 years. Rent climbs about 3% a year. Your $3,500 rent grows to nearly $4,700/month by year 10 and keeps going; your P&I stays frozen at $5,057 — the same check you wrote on day one. Every year the gap quietly closes in the owner's favor. Inflation is the renter's relentless adversary and the homeowner's quiet ally.

The bonus move: buy a duplex, let a tenant pay your mortgage.

Everything above assumed you live in the whole home. Now buy a duplex, live in one unit, and rent the other for $2,000/month. Same $1,000,000 price — but because it's owner-occupied, you can put as little as 5% down: just $50,000. Your gross carry is about $8,928/month, but the tenant immediately offsets $2,000, dropping your net effective cost to $6,928/month. You're controlling the same $1,000,000 asset with a quarter of the cash — and this is what makes the duplex the most powerful move in the analysis.

So what's the smart move?

The math here is not a coin flip. With the same starting capital, the homeowner's leveraged equity significantly outpaces the renter's stock portfolio at every horizon we modeled. Owning a home isn't the only path to wealth — a disciplined investor can build real assets — but for most people, who won't actually invest the difference every month for ten years, owning wins because it makes the right financial behavior automatic. The wrong question is "rent or own?" in the abstract. The right question is: what does the right price, right neighborhood, and right structure look like for your cash and timeline?
