Moving to LA from San Francisco — keep the taxes, lose half the price.

Leaving the Bay but not California? It's one of the cleanest moves there is: your taxes don't change at all — only the price of a home and the number of sunny days. Here's the honest 2026 math, and where SF transplants land in Los Angeles.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Updated July 12, 2026 · 7 min read

You love California. You're just tired of paying San Francisco prices for a foggy one-bedroom and watching the AI boom push the median home toward $2 million. Here's the good news for Bay Area folks eyeing Los Angeles: this is one of the cleanest moves you can make — because the thing everyone worries about, taxes, doesn't change at all.

You stay in California. Same state income tax, same Proposition 13, same rules. What changes is the price of a home and the number of sunny days. Let's look at the real 2026 numbers.

Same dollar, a lot more home.

This is the headline. San Francisco's median home price sits around $1.7 million in 2026 — with the AI-driven luxury market pushing records near $2M — and that typically buys a condo or a compact row house. Across Los Angeles County, the median is closer to $900,000. The same Bay Area budget can put you in a freestanding house with a yard and a garage, often with hundreds of thousands left over.

The honest caveat: LA's prime beach and Westside blocks — Santa Monica, Venice, the canyons — can still rival San Francisco dollar for dollar. But across the city, your money simply buys more square footage and outdoor space: roughly 1,800 square feet of single-family home for the price of an 1,100-square-foot SF condo.

The rent gap.

Renting first? A median one-bedroom runs about $3,600/month in San Francisco versus roughly $2,550 in LA — about $1,050 a month back in your pocket, or some $12,000 a year. And while SF rents have been climbing, LA's have softened slightly, widening the gap.

The best part: your taxes don't move.

People relocating between states agonize over tax changes. You don't have to. Because both cities are in California, your state income tax (up to 13.3% at the top), your Prop 13 property-tax basis rules, and your capital-gains treatment are identical. Neither San Francisco nor Los Angeles charges a separate city income tax. The only thing the move lowers is your cost of living. (Rates approximate, as of 2026; not tax advice.)

Your tax picture doesn't change — only your cost of housing and your weather do.

Yes, there's tech in LA.

The biggest myth Bay Area people carry south is that LA isn't a tech town. It is. Culver City, Playa Vista, and Santa Monica — collectively "Silicon Beach" — host major tech and entertainment-tech employers, and plenty of Bay Area companies run LA offices. Add entertainment, aerospace, and media, and the job market is broader than the Bay's mono-industry intensity.

The lifestyle shift, honestly.

You'll trade Karl the Fog for reliable sun, hills and microclimates for sprawl and freeways, and a compact city for a vast one. Some things you'll miss — the walkability, the transit, the density. But you'll gain space, warmth, a real coastline, and a home that doesn't require winning a bidding war over a $1.8M fixer. For a lot of Bay Area transplants, LA feels like California with room to breathe.

SF → LA at a glance — mid-2026

  • Median home price: ~$1.7M (SF, highs near $2M) vs ~$900K (LA County).
  • Median 1-bed rent: ~$3,600 (SF) vs ~$2,550 (LA) — about $1,050/mo less.
  • Taxes: no change — both California; neither city has an income tax.
  • Tech work: Silicon Beach (Culver City, Playa Vista, Santa Monica) is a real landing pad.
