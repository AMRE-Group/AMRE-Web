Which mortgage is right for your LA purchase?

Los Angeles prices mean most buyers here carry loan amounts well above national averages — and the loan that fits a $450K condo in Phoenix is often the wrong one for a $1.1M duplex in Culver City. This is a plain-English guide to the mortgage structures LA buyers actually use, what each costs, and when each one makes sense.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Apr 6, 2026 · 5 min read

In Los Angeles, loan amounts routinely exceed national averages, so the mortgage structure you choose — before you're in escrow — is one of the most valuable decisions you'll make. Five categories cover almost every LA purchase. Here's how they compare, and who each one fits.

Conventional loans (under $1,089,300).

The most common mortgage — meeting Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines and carrying the lowest rates available to most buyers. In LA County the conforming limit is $1,089,300. Minimum down is 3–5% for primary residences (10–25% for investment properties). Private mortgage insurance applies below 20% down — typically 0.5–1.5% of the loan annually, or roughly $375–1,125/month on a $900K loan — which often makes the case for a slightly larger down payment if you have it.

Jumbo loans (over $1,089,300).

Any loan above the conforming limit is a jumbo — the primary category for a large share of LA purchases. Rates typically run 0.25–0.5% higher than conforming, though the gap narrows for strong borrowers. Expect 10–20% down, a 700+ credit score, 12–18 months of reserves, and a lower debt-to-income ceiling. Because jumbos are held by individual banks, each sets its own guidelines — so shopping matters far more here: lender variance of 0.375–0.75% is $7,500–$15,000 a year on a $2M loan.

Jumbo is the majority of our buyer clients at AMRE — and it's the one loan type where shopping lenders hard genuinely pays for itself.

FHA loans.

Government-backed, with lenient qualification — credit floors around 580, higher allowable DTI, and down payments as low as 3.5%. The LA County FHA limit matches conforming at $1,089,300. The trade-off is insurance: a 1.75% upfront premium plus monthly MIP for the life of the loan (about $15,750 upfront and $700–800/month on a $900K loan), removable only by refinancing into a conventional loan at 20%+ equity. Best for first-time buyers prioritizing entry over minimizing ongoing cost. Note that in multiple-offer situations, some sellers resist FHA offers over appraisal and timeline concerns — worth planning with your agent.

Portfolio & bank-statement loans.

Held entirely by the originating lender and never sold to Fannie/Freddie, so the lender writes its own rules — invaluable for buyers who don't fit the conventional mold:

Who portfolio loans serve

  • Self-employed buyers whose returns understate real earnings — qualified on 12–24 months of bank statements instead of tax returns.
  • High-net-worth buyers with assets but irregular income — asset-depletion loans qualify income by dividing assets by the loan term.
  • Foreign nationals without US credit history — several lenders run specific programs.
  • Buyers with recent credit events — some lenders work with a bankruptcy or short sale as recent as two years back.

The cost is a rate roughly 0.5–1.5% higher than conforming or jumbo — the price of flexibility, and for LA's large self-employed population, often the difference between buying and not.

TIC & fractional financing.

Tenancy-in-Common (TIC) properties — a growing LA ownership structure — require specific financing because you're buying a fractional interest in a building, not a discrete unit. Standard conventional and jumbo lenders typically won't finance TICs. Specialist TIC lenders offer loans up to 85% LTV, fixed or interest-only, usually 0.5–1% above comparable conventional rates — a premium the lower TIC purchase price often more than offsets. FAQ

LA mortgages, answered.

What is the conforming loan limit in Los Angeles for 2026?

In LA County the conforming loan limit is $1,089,300. Borrow up to that amount and you can still qualify for a conventional loan — the category with the lowest rates for most buyers. Anything above it is a jumbo loan.

When do LA buyers need a jumbo loan?

Any loan above the $1,089,300 conforming limit is a jumbo loan — the primary category for a large share of LA purchases, especially the $1.5M–$5M Westside range. Jumbo rates typically run 0.25–0.5% higher than conforming, but the gap narrows for well-qualified borrowers, and lender variance is wide, so shopping matters.

Can you use an FHA loan in Los Angeles?

Yes. The FHA limit in LA County is $1,089,300, with down payments as low as 3.5% and credit floors around 580. The trade-off is mortgage insurance: a 1.75% upfront premium plus monthly MIP for the life of the loan, removable only by refinancing into a conventional loan at 20%+ equity.

What mortgage do self-employed LA buyers use?

Often a portfolio or bank-statement loan. These are held by the originating lender rather than sold to Fannie/Freddie, so the lender sets its own rules and can qualify income from 12–24 months of bank statements or from assets. They run about 0.5–1.5% above conforming rates — the price of the flexibility.

How do you finance a TIC in Los Angeles?

Tenancy-in-Common (TIC) purchases require specialist fractional financing because you're buying a share of a building, not a discrete unit. Specialist TIC lenders offer loans up to 85% LTV, fixed or interest-only, typically 0.5–1% above comparable conventional rates — a premium the lower TIC purchase price often offsets.
