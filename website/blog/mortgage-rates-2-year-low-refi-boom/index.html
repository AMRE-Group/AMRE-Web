Mortgage Rates Hit a 2-Year Low, Sparking a Refi Boom.

What falling mortgage rates mean for buyers waiting on the sidelines — and for current homeowners who may finally have an opening to refinance into something better.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · Apr 3, 2026 · 4 min read

Mortgage demand is surging. Weekly applications jumped 14% as the average 30-year fixed rate hit its lowest point in two years — and homeowners who bought at higher rates are moving fast. Here are the numbers behind the surge, and what they mean whether you own or you're still shopping.

Rates drop to 6.15%.

The average interest rate for a 30-year fixed mortgage fell to 6.15%, down from 6.29% the previous week — the lowest reading in two years. In a market as price-heavy as Los Angeles, even a small rate move changes the monthly math meaningfully: on an $800K loan, each percentage point is worth roughly $530 a month.

Refinance applications skyrocket.

Homeowners jumped at the chance to lock in lower rates, with refinancing applications up 24% from the previous week — and 127% higher than the same week last year. Most of these homeowners bought when rates were higher, so the savings are immediate and substantial. For the broader rate picture, see our market & rate update.

Purchase demand is stirring too.

Applications for purchase mortgages rose 5% on the week. Lower rates and slower home-price growth are improving affordability at the margin — and history says that when rates fall meaningfully, sidelined buyers return quickly and competition follows. Buyers weighing the own-vs-rent question can start with our rent-vs-own analysis at $1M and the primer on the benefits of homeownership.

What's driving the surge.

The move comes as the Federal Reserve is expected to cut interest rates. Mortgage rates don't follow the Fed's overnight rate directly — they track the 10-year Treasury and market expectations — which is why rates fell ahead of the decision. Expectations are already priced in; what happens next depends on the Fed's guidance and the economic data that follows. Volatility cuts both ways, which argues for locking a good rate when you see one rather than betting on the bottom. How to tell if your refi pencils.

01

Check your rate gap

A refinance typically starts to make sense when you can cut your rate by 0.75–1 point. The bigger your balance, the smaller the gap needs to be.

02

Get real quotes

Collect at least two full loan estimates — rate, points, and total closing costs. Lender pricing varies more than most borrowers expect.

03

Find your break-even

Divide closing costs by monthly savings. If you'll stay in the home past that month count, the refi pencils. If not, keep your current loan.

Mortgage rate FAQs.

When does refinancing make sense?

When you can cut your rate by roughly 0.75–1 point and you'll stay in the home past the break-even point — the month your cumulative savings exceed the refi's closing costs.

Do mortgage rates follow Fed rate cuts?

Not directly. They track the 10-year Treasury and market expectations, which is why mortgage rates often fall before a Fed cut and can even rise after one.

Should I wait for lower rates to buy?

Falling rates bring competition back and push prices up. Many buyers do better purchasing when the field is thin — and refinancing later if rates keep falling.

How much does 1% of rate save in LA?

On an $800,000 loan, about $530 a month — roughly $6,400 a year — which is why small rate moves trigger big refi waves in high-priced markets.
