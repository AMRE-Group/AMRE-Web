35 years of Los Angeles home appreciation, in one chart.

From a median of $124,800 in 1989 to $906,000 in 2024, Los Angeles home values surged 626% — about 5.3% a year — through every recession, bubble, and recovery. Here is the full picture.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · February 8, 2026 · 5 min read

The Los Angeles housing market has delivered tremendous long-term appreciation. From a median home price of $124,800 in 1989 to $906,000 in 2024, values have surged 626% — more than 7x in nominal terms. The trajectory has been unmistakably upward, but far from smooth: the market has moved through booms, corrections, and recoveries driven by major economic events.

Three and a half decades, five distinct cycles.

1989–1999

Recession & slow growth

Aerospace layoffs and high rates drove prices down 3–9% a year from 1991–1996 before the late-90s recovery.

2000–2012

Boom & bust

Easy credit fueled a 164% run-up to $585K by 2006 — then the subprime crash erased 44% of values by 2012.

2013–2024

Recovery & pandemic surge

Steady 5–8% annual growth, then a nearly 40% pandemic-era climb to a record $906K median.

1. The 1990s recession and slow growth (1989–1999)

After steady appreciation in the 1980s, the market hit a rough patch in the early-1990s recession. High unemployment, aerospace industry layoffs, and rising interest rates led to several years of price declines — between 1991 and 1996, median prices fell 3–9% per year, wiping out previous gains. As the economy recovered in the late 1990s, values began climbing steadily, setting the stage for the early-2000s boom.

2. The early-2000s housing boom (2000–2006)

Low interest rates, easy lending (including subprime mortgages), and speculative buying fueled an unprecedented surge. Between 2000 and 2006, LA home prices jumped 164%, from $221,000 to $585,000 — one of the fastest growth periods on record, with annual appreciation frequently topping 15–20% and some years exceeding 25%. The growth was unsustainable, driven largely by risky lending.

3. The subprime crisis and housing crash (2007–2012)

The bubble burst in 2007, producing the most significant price decline in Los Angeles history. As subprime mortgages defaulted and foreclosures spiked, home values plummeted 44% from 2007 to 2012 — with 2008 alone dropping 37%. The market bottomed in 2011 below $320,000, and full recovery took nearly 12 years.

4. Post-crisis recovery and steady growth (2013–2019)

As the economy rebounded and distressed properties were absorbed, the market entered a new growth phase — rising at an average 5–8% per year and surpassing $600,000 by decade's end. Unlike the 2000s boom, this growth was driven by low inventory, strong demand, and stricter lending standards rather than speculation.

5. The pandemic housing boom (2020–2024)

Record-low mortgage rates and a surge in demand for space triggered an unexpected boom: prices soared nearly 40% between 2020 and 2024, with 2021 alone posting a record 25% gain. Rising rates cooled the pace by 2022–2023, but values held firm well above pre-pandemic levels. For where the market stands today, see our mid-year 2026 LA market update.

Leverage changes everything.

The S&P 500 beat real estate on pure price appreciation. But leverage flips the equation: a Los Angeles home purchased with 20% down in 1989 generated roughly a 3,130% return on the initial cash invested by 2024, versus 1,271% for the S&P 500 — because appreciation applies to the entire property value, not just your down payment. That lifts the effective CAGR on invested cash from 5.83% to 10.44%, ahead of the stock market's 7.77%.

Real estate carries higher transaction costs, ongoing expenses, and illiquidity; stocks offer easier diversification and passive growth. But leveraged property adds rental income, tax benefits, and inflation protection — which is why the long-term LA owner has historically done so well. The right mix depends on your goals, risk tolerance, and timeline.

Key takeaways

  • Massive long-term growth: 626% over 35 years — a 5.3% compound annual growth rate.
  • Boom & bust cycles: prices surged in the early 2000s, dropped 44% in 2007–2012, then rebounded to record highs.
  • Pandemic spike: values climbed nearly 40% from 2020 to 2024.
  • Leverage matters: 20% down in 1989 turned into a ~3,130% return on cash by 2024 — well ahead of the S&P 500.
  • Resilient asset: every downturn in 35 years has eventually given way to new highs.

Despite economic swings, Los Angeles real estate has remained a substantial long-term investment. Downturns have temporarily lowered prices, but the market has rebounded to new highs every time. With continued demand, limited supply, and a growing economy, LA remains a premier market for long-term appreciation — whether you're buying your first home or deciding when to sell.

FAQ

Appreciation questions, answered.

How much have Los Angeles home prices risen over the last 35 years?

From 1989 to 2024, the LA median home price rose from $124,800 to $906,000 — a 626% increase, more than 7x in nominal terms, despite the 1990s recession and the 2008 crash.

What is the average annual home price appreciation in Los Angeles?

Roughly 5.3% per year compounded over the 35 years from 1989 to 2024 — through every boom, correction, and recovery.

What is the 10-year appreciation rate in LA County?

Over the most recent decade of the dataset, LA home values roughly doubled — steady 5–8% annual growth from 2013 to 2019, followed by a nearly 40% surge between 2020 and 2024. That works out to roughly 7% a year over the 10-year window.

Did Los Angeles home prices fall during the 2008 crash?

Yes — about 44% from 2007 to 2012, the steepest decline in the city's modern history, with 2008 alone dropping 37%. Prices bottomed in 2011 below $320,000 and took roughly 12 years to fully recover.

Is Los Angeles real estate a good long-term investment?

Historically, yes — especially with leverage. A home bought with 20% down in 1989 returned about 3,130% on the initial cash by 2024, versus roughly 1,271% for the S&P 500. Talk to AMRE about what that means for your situation today.
