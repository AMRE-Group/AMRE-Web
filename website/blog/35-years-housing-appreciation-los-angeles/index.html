35 Years of Home Appreciation in Los Angeles.

From a median of $124,800 in 1989 to $906,000 in 2024, LA home values rose 626% — an average annual appreciation rate of 5.3% through every boom, crash, and recovery.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · Feb 8, 2026 · 5 min read

The Los Angeles housing market has delivered tremendous long-term appreciation. From a median home price of $124,800 in 1989 to roughly $906,000 in 2024, values surged 626% — more than 7x in nominal terms. The trajectory has been upward, but far from a straight line: LA moved through booms, corrections, and recoveries driven by major economic events.

The market cycles behind the trend.

That 5.3% average annual rate masks five very different chapters. Understanding them is the difference between reacting to a headline and understanding how home price appreciation actually compounds over a full cycle.

1. The 1990s recession & slow growth (1989–1999)

After steady appreciation in the 1980s, the LA market hit a rough patch in the early-1990s recession. High unemployment, aerospace layoffs, and rising interest rates produced several years of price declines. Between 1991 and 1996, median prices fell 3–9% per year, wiping out prior gains. As the economy recovered late in the decade, values began to climb again — setting the stage for the early-2000s boom.

2. The early-2000s housing boom (2000–2006)

The early 2000s saw an unprecedented surge fueled by low interest rates, easy lending (including subprime mortgages), and speculative buying. Between 2000 and 2006, LA home prices jumped 164% — from $221,000 to $585,000 — one of the fastest growth periods in history, with some years topping 25% annual appreciation. But that growth was unsustainable, driven largely by risky lending.

3. The subprime crisis & crash (2007–2012)

The bubble burst in 2007, producing the most significant price decline in LA history. As subprime mortgages defaulted and foreclosures spiked, values plummeted 44% from 2007 to 2012 — with 2008 alone dropping about 37%. The market bottomed near $320,000 in 2011, levels last seen in the early 2000s, and took nearly a decade to fully recover.

4. Post-crisis recovery & steady growth (2013–2019)

As the economy rebounded and distressed inventory was absorbed, LA entered a new growth phase, rising a steady 5–8% per year and surpassing $600,000 by the decade's end. Unlike the early-2000s boom, this run was driven by low inventory, strong demand, and stricter lending standards rather than speculation.

5. The pandemic boom (2020–2024)

COVID-19 triggered an unexpected boom on record-low mortgage rates and surging demand for space. Prices climbed nearly 40% between 2020 and 2024, with 2021 alone posting a record ~25% gain. By 2022–2023, rising rates and affordability concerns cooled the market, but values held well above pre-pandemic levels — Los Angeles real estate stayed firm into 2024.

How LA compares nationally.

Long-run U.S. home appreciation runs roughly 4–5% nominal per year. LA's ~5.3% average edges out the national trend thanks to chronic supply constraints and sustained demand — though with sharper booms and busts along the way. For the national picture, see our companion piece on national home price appreciation since 1990.

Despite two major downturns, LA home prices compounded at about 5.3% a year for 35 years — rewarding owners who held through full market cycles.

Real estate vs. the stock market: the leverage effect.

While the S&P 500 has outperformed housing in pure price appreciation, leverage changes the equation. An LA home bought with 20% down in 1989 would have generated roughly a 3,130% return on the initial cash invested by 2024, versus about 1,271% for the S&P 500 — because appreciation applies to the entire property value, not just the cash down. That lifts the leveraged CAGR from 5.83% to about 10.44%, outpacing the market's 7.77%.

Housing carries higher transaction costs, ongoing expenses, and illiquidity. Stocks offer easy diversification and passive growth; real estate offers rental income, tax benefits, and inflation protection. The right choice depends on your goals, timeline, and risk tolerance.

Key takeaways

  • Massive long-term growth: +626% over 35 years — a 5.3% average annual appreciation rate.
  • 10-year gain: roughly +93% from 2014 to 2024 (~6.8% a year).
  • 20-year gain: about +166% (~5% a year), even including the 2008 crash.
  • Boom & bust: +164% in the early 2000s, −44% in the 2007–2012 crash, then record highs.
  • Edges the nation: LA's ~5.3% beats the ~4–5% U.S. long-run average.
