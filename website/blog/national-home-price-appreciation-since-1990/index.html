Home price appreciation since 1990 — the long view.

US median home prices are up 327% since 1990, against 141% inflation. Three decades of real estate data — booms, busts, and recoveries — and what the long game actually pays patient owners.

The U.S. housing market has experienced significant shifts over the past three decades, particularly in single-family homes. Examining the change in median sales prices since 1990 gives valuable insight into market trends shaped by economic cycles and major events — and a clear answer to the question buyers keep asking: how much do home prices actually go up over time?

Long-term growth vs. inflation.

Since 1990, the median sales price of single-family homes in the U.S. has increased by 327%, surpassing the inflation rate of 141% over the same period. This growth highlights real estate's effectiveness as a long-term investment, often yielding returns significantly above inflation — the same pattern we see locally in 35 years of Los Angeles appreciation data.

How much homes appreciate per decade.

At the long-run average of roughly 4–5% a year, home values compound to roughly 50–60% per decade — but no single decade is average. Here's the cumulative price growth measured from each starting point through 2023:

The critical periods, decoded.

Pandemic surge (2020–2023): prices rose 38%, driven by low rates and intense demand — a period of bidding wars and affordability strain. Post-recession steady growth (2015–2023): an 84% climb as the recovery matured and millennials entered the market, delivering major equity gains. Recession recovery (2010–2023): a 134% rebound from the Great Recession floor, underscoring the market's resilience. Boom and bust (2000–2023): 184% growth that includes both the early-2000s bubble and its collapse — a reminder that speculative peaks correct, and long holders still came out well ahead.

And now: over the past year, median prices rose a modest 4% — a cooling market shaped by affordability limits and higher rates. Buyers are more selective; sellers are adjusting expectations.

Why owners earn more than the headline rate.

Because most buyers purchase with a mortgage, the return on the actual cash invested is far higher than the appreciation rate itself. That's leverage — and it's why the long-run numbers matter so much for wealth building:

Key takeaways

  • US median home prices are up 327% since 1990 — more than double the 141% inflation over the period.
  • Long-run appreciation averages ~4–5% a year, roughly 50–60% per decade compounded.
  • Every major downturn since 1990 — including the Great Recession — was recovered and surpassed.
  • Leverage means the return on your invested cash typically far exceeds the headline appreciation rate.

The data since 1990 reveals the U.S. housing market's resilience and capacity for long-term growth. Despite downturns and corrections, real estate has consistently outperformed inflation and rewarded patient owners. Frequently asked questions.

How much do house prices go up every decade on average?

Based on US median prices since 1990, homes appreciate roughly 4–5% a year on average — about 50–60% per decade compounded. Individual decades vary widely: the 2010s recovery ran far hotter, while the Great Recession years saw declines before recovering.

How much have US home prices appreciated since 1990?

About 327% through 2023, versus roughly 141% cumulative inflation over the same stretch.

Does real estate beat inflation?

Historically, yes — and because most owners buy with a mortgage, the return on actual cash invested is typically far higher than the headline appreciation rate.

Is real estate still a good long-term investment in 2026?

Recent growth has cooled to about 4% a year, but the 30+ year record shows patient owners consistently rewarded through recessions, rate spikes, and corrections — especially once leverage is factored in.
