Buying a Duplex in Los Angeles. The house-hacking math.

Live in one unit, rent the other, and let a tenant help pay your mortgage. Owner-occupied duplex financing plus LA appreciation is a combination that's hard to replicate with any other real estate strategy — here's exactly how the numbers work.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Updated July 8, 2026 · 5 min read

Buying a duplex is one of the smartest ways to enter the Los Angeles real estate market — and the reason is simple. When you live in one unit and rent the other, you get primary-residence financing (low down payment, best rates) on a property that also produces income. A tenant helps carry the mortgage while the entire building appreciates in your name. Let's walk through a real example.

A real LA duplex, run the numbers.

Take 1423 W 65th St, Los Angeles, CA 90047 — a fully remodeled duplex listed at $799,900. It offers two separate units (each with its own address, gas, and electrical meters), 1,541 sq. ft. of total living space, a new roof with fully paid-off solar panels, a brand-new detached two-car garage, and updated landscaping with new irrigation. It's a short commute to SoFi Stadium, Downtown LA, the Arts District, and Metro Link stations. In other words: a livable, rentable, appreciating asset in a well-located pocket of the city.

Why the duplex builds wealth.

Three forces stack up at once. First, dual income: renting one unit generates cash flow that offsets the mortgage and gives you a buffer for unexpected costs. Second, appreciation: LA real estate has historically appreciated steadily, and at roughly 7% a year this $799,900 property could be worth about $1,573,130 in ten years. Third, equity: every payment pays down principal while the value climbs, boosting both your net worth and your borrowing power for the next investment.

Projected value at 7% annual appreciation

A $799,900 duplex could nearly double in a decade — before counting rental income or principal paydown.

Over ten years, renting one unit at $2,000/month could bring in roughly $240,000 in rental income — money that helps cover the mortgage and lifts your return. Combine that with appreciation and principal paydown and the example duplex points to a potential net gain of over $1,023,671, a headline ROI around 581.5%, and an estimated 10–12% IRR across the hold. The early years can show negative cash-on-cash returns because the payments are high, but equity growth and appreciation are what make the long-run numbers work.

Key takeaways

  • Primary-residence leverage is the whole edge — you finance a rental-producing asset like a home, not an investment property.
  • A tenant offsets your cost: ~$2,000/month can drop your net payment on the example duplex to about $3,132.
  • Appreciation compounds on the full asset, not just your down payment — ~7%/yr points to ~$1.57M in ten years.
  • Ideal for first-time investors: more affordable and easier to finance than larger multifamily buildings.
