Do you need a buyer's agent in Los Angeles?

The short answer: no law requires one. The longer answer is why almost every buyer still wants representation — and how the new written buyer-broker agreement actually works in 2026.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Jun 12, 2026 · 6 min read

Let's answer the question directly: no, you are not legally required to use a buyer's agent to buy a home in Los Angeles. But if you've started touring homes recently, you may have been handed a form to sign before anyone would unlock a door. That's new, and it trips people up. So before we get to whether you need an agent, here's what actually changed — and why representation still pays.

What changed in 2024–2026.

A 2024 national legal settlement (the NAR settlement) and California's own Assembly Bill 2992 reshaped how buyer representation begins. A licensed agent must now have a signed, written buyer-representation agreement before showing you any property. Buyer-agent compensation also no longer appears on the MLS — instead, it's negotiated.

None of this means buyer representation got worse. If anything it got clearer. The agreement spells out exactly what your agent will do, for how long, and how they're paid — in writing, before the work starts. The old handshake-and-good-faith version left all of that fuzzy.

Who actually pays the buyer's agent?

This is the question on everyone's mind, and the honest answer is: it depends, but you often pay less than you'd think. The buyer is responsible for the fee under the agreement, but the buyer's agent can request that the seller cover it as a concession in the offer — which is still common in Los Angeles real estate.

So in practice, many LA buyers still pay little or nothing toward their agent out of pocket — the cost is folded into the deal. What's different is that it's now explicit instead of automatic. A good agent tells you up front how their fee works, how they'll try to get the seller to cover it, and what happens if the seller won't. If anyone is vague about money, that's your signal to ask harder questions — or find a different agent. We break down the full picture in who pays the real estate agent in Los Angeles.

What a great buyer's agent actually does.

The fee conversation tends to overshadow the real question: what are you getting for it? A skilled buyer's agent is doing far more than unlocking doors. They're running a parallel process across search, valuation, negotiation, due diligence, and contracts — the parts of a purchase where mistakes are expensive and hard to undo.

The architect-agent edge.

Most buyers' agents can tell you what a home costs. Fewer can tell you what it would cost to fix, change, or add on. As a licensed architect who is also a licensed Realtor, I read each property structurally: what's cosmetic versus expensive, what a wall or a permit actually means, and what a home could become.

It's a different kind of representation: not just "is this a good price?" but "is this a good building?" That read is baked into how we work with buyers, and it's exactly the perspective that keeps a smart purchase from becoming an expensive surprise.

The real question isn't whether you need an agent. It's whether the agent you choose will actually protect you — on price, on paper, and on the building itself.

5 things to check before you sign any buyer agreement.

Because the agreement now comes early, read it before you sign — you have every right to ask for changes. Here's what to look at:

Before you sign, confirm:

  • Term length. California caps each term at 90 days. Shorter is fine, and you can renew.
  • How the agent is paid. The fee should be stated clearly, along with the plan to ask the seller to cover it.
  • Exclusive vs. non-exclusive. Know whether you're committing to one agent or keeping your options open.
  • Scope. Broad representation or a single-property agreement — both are allowed.
  • Cancellation. Understand how and when you can end the agreement if it isn't working.

The bottom line.

You're not required to hire a buyer's agent in Los Angeles — but going without one means representing yourself against a seller who has a professional in their corner. The 2026 rules didn't make representation worse; they made it clearer. Choose an agent who's transparent about money and sharp about the property itself. Frequently asked questions

Do you need a buyer's agent in Los Angeles?

You're not legally required to use one, but in practice almost every buyer benefits. A buyer's agent represents your interests, not the seller's. As of 2026, if you want a licensed agent to show you homes, California law requires a signed written buyer-broker agreement before they can take you on a tour.

Who pays the buyer's agent commission in California?

Buyer-agent compensation is now negotiated rather than posted on the MLS. The buyer is responsible for it under the agreement, but the agent can request that the seller pay it as a concession in the offer — still common in Los Angeles. In many transactions the seller ultimately covers the fee, so buyers often pay little or nothing out of pocket.

What is a buyer-broker agreement?

It's a signed written contract that spells out what your agent will do, for how long, and how they're paid. Since the 2024 NAR settlement and California's AB 2992, a licensed agent must have one before showing you any property. Each term can't exceed 90 days, and you can negotiate a shorter term or a single-property agreement.

Can you buy a home in LA without a buyer's agent?

Yes, but you'd be representing yourself against a seller who has professional representation. A buyer's agent finds and vets homes, advises on value, negotiates terms, manages due diligence, and protects you contractually — so most buyers are better served with one.
