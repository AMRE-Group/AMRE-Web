Your net proceeds, explained.

The number that matters isn't your sale price — it's what actually lands in your account after commission, payoff, and closing costs. Here's how that number is built, and the five factors that move it the most.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, REALTOR® · Jul 8, 2026 · 6 min read

Every seller fixates on one number: the sale price. It's the number on the sign, the number in the offer, the number your neighbors will ask about. But it's not the number that actually lands in your account.

That number is your net proceeds — sale price, minus everything that gets paid out of escrow before the rest is wired to you. Understanding how it's built is the difference between a seller who's pleasantly surprised at closing and one who isn't.

What net proceeds actually means.

The formula is simple, even if the line items aren't:

Net Proceeds = Sale Price − Commission − Mortgage Payoff − Closing Costs

Commission is usually the largest deduction — typically 5–6% total, split between the listing and buyer's agents. Mortgage payoff is whatever you still owe your lender. And “closing costs” is really a bundle of smaller items: city and county transfer taxes, your owner's title policy, escrow and settlement fees, and a handful of smaller charges like recording fees and a home warranty.

The top 5 factors that move your sale price.

Net proceeds starts with sale price — and sale price isn't fixed. It's the result of decisions sellers make, or don't make, in the weeks before listing. Here are the five that move it most.

1. Pricing strategy

The most expensive mistake sellers make is pricing to what they want rather than what the data supports. An overpriced listing in LA typically sits for 30+ days, picks up a “stale” signal, and often closes below where a correctly priced listing would have — with more stress and more time invested. Price with a real CMA, not sentiment.

2. Presentation & staging

Staged homes in LA consistently sell faster and at higher prices than unstaged equivalents at the same price point. The ROI is reliably positive — typically $3–5 returned for every $1 spent on furniture and design.

3. Marketing exposure

Roughly 97% of buyers start their search online — the photos are the first showing. Professional photography, video, and a phased marketing strategy (like Compass's Private Exclusive window before public MLS launch) create early competition and urgency instead of a listing that opens to silence.

4. Condition & pre-listing prep

Unpermitted work, deferred maintenance, and disclosure surprises don't just risk the sale — they hand buyers negotiating leverage after you're already in escrow. Addressing these before you list, ideally on the 90-day pre-listing timeline, protects the price you actually agreed to.

5. Timing

Spring (February through May) historically sees the highest buyer activity in LA, and Thursday is typically the strongest day to go live — buyers search over the weekend while your listing is at its freshest. Broader conditions like rates and inventory matter too, but well-priced homes move in every season.

Every one of these factors flows straight into the number that matters: the check you receive at closing. A higher sale price only helps if it isn't consumed by extra weeks on market, an avoidable price reduction, or costs you didn't plan for — the difference between a good offer and the right offer.

Key takeaways

  • Net Proceeds = Sale Price − Commission − Mortgage Payoff − Closing Costs.
  • Commission (typically 5–6%) is usually the largest single deduction.
  • LA sellers also owe ~0.56% combined transfer tax, plus escrow and title fees.
  • Five levers move your sale price: pricing, staging, marketing, condition, and timing.
