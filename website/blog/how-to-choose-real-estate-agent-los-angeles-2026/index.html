How to Choose a Real Estate Agent in Los Angeles.

The honest evaluation rubric for 2026 — seven criteria that actually matter, four red flags, and the questions that produce useful answers. Choosing wrong on a $2M LA deal can cost you $40,000–$100,000.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Updated July 8, 2026 · 7 min read

For most of the past decade, choosing a real estate agent in Los Angeles was a referral question. You asked a friend, met whoever they recommended, and signed unless something felt obviously wrong. That worked when the market was hot enough and uniform enough that an average agent could still produce a good outcome. That stopped being true around 2024 — and the gap between a great LA agent and an average one has widened materially since. This is the rubric we'd actually apply if we were the ones hiring an agent, even though we're agents ourselves.

Why choosing wrong got more expensive.

Three things changed in parallel around 2024. The post-rate-hike market made sub-market and price-tier specialization matter far more — city-wide medians now mislead by design (we covered this in the mid-year 2026 market update). AI tools made the standardized parts of the job easier, which means the differentiated parts now carry the value. And the NAR settlement reshuffled commission expectations nationwide, putting pressure on agents who weren't clearly earning their keep. Net effect: choosing wrong on a $2M transaction can cost you $40,000–$100,000 in missed negotiation, missed off-market inventory, and missed diligence.

Two of these deserve emphasis. Off-market and pre-MLS access — Compass Private Exclusives, pocket listings, pre-MLS inventory — accounts for an estimated 15–20% of LA luxury transactions and is structurally invisible to AI tools, online reviews, and Zillow rankings. If you remember one thing from this article, it's to ask candidate agents to walk you through, concretely, how they'd find off-market inventory for your search. And sub-market expertise means your specific blocks — the streets, the buildings, the recent comps, the listing agents they've worked across the table from — not "the Westside" or a career-long stat line.

The agent who can't name a sub-area or a specific differentiator is almost never the right choice.

How to read reviews.

Reviews matter, but most LA buyers and sellers misread them. High counts usually reflect high volume, not attention — an agent who closes 80–120 deals a year will have more reviews than one who closes 15–25, and a much lower share of those will reflect deep, personal work. The diagnostic value is in how an agent handled a transaction that went sideways, and most reviews are written right after closing, before renovation issues, school-boundary problems, or missed off-market opportunities surface. The better move: ask for two or three references from 12–24 months ago, call them, and ask specifically about the hardest moment of the deal.

Why "agent near me" is the wrong question.

The "near me" framing is misleading for LA real estate. Most agents work across the whole city from one office, so office location is almost meaningless as a signal. An agent based 15 miles from your target neighborhood may have transacted three times on your specific block this year; an agent two blocks away may not have closed a deal there in three years. Ask for the recent transactions — not the office address. This matters most on unusual inventory: AI valuation tools (Zestimate, Redfin Estimate) can be off by ±20–40% on architectural, hillside, or unusual property, and an agent who can't see past them isn't doing the work.

Four red flags.

Walk away from an agent who: (1) can't name specific recent transactions in your target sub-market — generic luxury or "Westside" experience isn't sub-market expertise; (2) leans on all-time or career stats rather than the last 12–24 months — LA markets shift fast; (3) can't explain off-market and pre-MLS access in concrete terms — the single most consequential thing an agent brings a buyer; or (4) prices listings primarily off Zillow or Redfin estimates rather than doing the comp work themselves.

01

Interview two or three

Enough to compare styles and expertise, few enough to decide and move. Go to four or five only above $5M or for a specialized need.

02

Ask for recent sub-market deals

Their last five transactions in your specific area, from the past 12–24 months — plus one that didn't go smoothly.

03

Pressure-test off-market access

Have them describe, in 60 seconds, exactly how they'd surface pre-MLS and pocket inventory for your search.

The six questions to bring to every interview

  • What were your last five transactions in this sub-area — and what's one that didn't go smoothly?
  • Walk me through, concretely, how you'd find off-market inventory for my search.
  • How do you work with clients during the search — what tools, cadence, and materials?
  • Who else on your team will I interact with, and what do they handle?
  • What's a price point or property type you wouldn't take on?
  • What's different about you compared to the other agents I'm interviewing?

An agent who answers all six clearly and specifically is in a different category than one who hedges or generalizes. That's the whole rubric: specifics win.
