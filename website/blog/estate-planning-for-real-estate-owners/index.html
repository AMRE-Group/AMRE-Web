Estate Planning for Real Estate Owners: Trusts & Taxes.

Your properties may be the most valuable things you own. Proper planning — usually built around a trust — ensures they're protected, managed, and passed on according to your wishes, not a probate court's schedule.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Feb 28, 2026 · 5 min read

Owning real estate — a family home, a rental, a commercial building — is a significant investment. Estate planning ensures those properties are protected, managed, and transferred the way you intend. The two decisions that matter most: which trust structure to use, and how taxes will treat it.

Revocable living trusts: control plus probate avoidance.

The revocable living trust is the workhorse of estate planning for property owners. Placing real estate in one lets it bypass probate — the slow, public, costly court process — so your beneficiaries receive it faster and more privately. You keep complete control: amend it, change beneficiaries, or dissolve it entirely while you're alive. Tax-wise, nothing changes during your lifetime; the property stays in your taxable estate and income flows through your personal return.

Irrevocable trusts: protection at a price.

An irrevocable trust permanently moves property out of your personal ownership. In exchange for giving up control, you gain asset protection — shielding the property from creditors, lawsuits, and divorce settlements — and potential estate-tax reduction, since the property is no longer part of your estate. The trade-off is real: the trust is a separate taxpayer with compressed brackets (top federal rate at roughly $15,000 of trust income), though income can be distributed to beneficiaries at their usually-lower rates.

The 2026 estate-tax landscape.

Under current law, the federal exemption is $15 million per individual — $30 million per married couple — beginning in 2026, with amounts above that taxed at up to 40%. California levies no state estate tax, but a dozen other states do, with far lower thresholds — relevant if you own property across state lines. Strategies like irrevocable trusts and lifetime gifting can reduce a taxable estate, and for investment property, a 1031 exchange paired with the step-up in basis at death remains one of the most powerful wealth-transfer combinations in real estate.

Key takeaways

  • A revocable living trust keeps control and skips probate — the default for most owners.
  • Irrevocable trusts trade control for creditor protection and estate-tax savings.
  • Current law sets the 2026 federal exemption at $15M per person; 40% applies above it.
  • Review your plan regularly — and always with a qualified estate-planning professional.
