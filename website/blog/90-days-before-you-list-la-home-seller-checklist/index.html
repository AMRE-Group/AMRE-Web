The 90 days before you list — an LA seller's checklist.

Most sellers come to market underprepared. The ones who get the best prices spend 90 days doing things most agents never mention — before anyone ever sees the home. Here's the playbook, in order.

Price still matters. Presentation still matters. But the foundation of a top-dollar result is built long before anyone ever sees your home. Here's the playbook.

Days 90–60: the decisions that affect your net.

Pull your permit history. Unpermitted work is one of the most common — and most expensive — surprises in an LA transaction. Pull your own records through the city or county portal before a buyer's inspector does. If there's unpermitted square footage, a garage conversion, or an ADU built without permits, you have three options: disclose and price accordingly, permit retroactively, or convert it back. None of those are fast decisions.

Order a preliminary title report. Liens, clouds on title, and easement issues surface here. A pre-listing title review costs roughly $150–200 and can save you weeks of escrow delays. If there's anything unusual, you want to know before buyers do.

Decide on Compass Concierge or your own contractor. If your home needs work — kitchen refresh, paint, landscaping, flooring — you have about 75 days to complete it before photography. The Compass Concierge program funds eligible improvements upfront with no interest, repaid at closing. Going DIY? Get bids now — good LA contractors are booked weeks out.

Get a pre-listing appraisal on higher-value homes. For homes likely to attract jumbo financing, a pre-listing appraisal sets realistic price expectations and arms you with documentation if a buyer's appraisal comes in low. Almost always useful above $2 million.

Days 60–30: preparation and positioning.

Complete all deferred maintenance. The checklist: HVAC service records and a tune-up if due, roof inspection and minor repairs, water-heater age check (buyers and lenders flag units over 10 years), exterior caulking and paint touch-ups, and a full check of all GFCIs, smoke detectors, and CO detectors. California law requires working smoke and CO detectors at transfer — a $15 item that shows up on every inspection report if missed.

Run your NHD report. Natural Hazard Disclosure reports are required in California and cover fire zones, flood zones, earthquake-fault proximity, and other mandatory disclosures. LA County has expanded high-fire-hazard zones significantly over the past five years. Know your zone before a buyer sees it.

Begin the staging conversation. Good LA stagers are booked 3–4 weeks out for occupied homes, longer for vacant ones. Staged homes consistently sell faster and at higher prices than unstaged equivalents at the same price point — a reliably positive ROI, typically $3–5 returned for every $1 spent.

Audit your HOA if applicable. Pull current financials, the past 12 months of meeting minutes, and any pending special assessments. Surprises — a large assessment, litigation in progress, a reserve fund below 10% — kill deals or force price reductions. Better to know now.

Days 30–0: the launch.

Professional photography is non-negotiable. Roughly 97% of buyers start their search online — the photos are the first showing. Budget for still photography, a video walkthrough, and 3D Matterport if the property is over $1.5M or has a complex layout. Drone photography earns its keep for views, large lots, or notable outdoor space.

Use the Private Exclusive window. Before public MLS launch, Compass's Private Exclusive program markets your home to 34,000+ Compass agents — generating early interest, gathering price-sensitivity intelligence, and creating urgency before days-on-market starts counting. This phase typically runs 1–2 weeks and belongs on virtually every listing.

Price with data, not sentiment. The most expensive mistake sellers make is pricing to what they want rather than what the data supports. An overpriced LA listing typically sits 30+ days, accumulates a "stale" signal, and ultimately closes below where a correctly priced listing would have. Your agent should provide a written CMA with genuine comparables — not a number designed to win the listing.

Time the market week, not just the season. In LA, Thursday is the strongest day to go live — buyers search over the weekend and your listing is freshest for Saturday showings. Spring (February–May) sees the highest activity, but well-priced inventory moves in every season. Don't wait for "the perfect time" if your property is ready now.

Key takeaways

  • Start 90 days out. Permits, title, and contractors all take time.
  • Fix, disclose, and stage before the camera ever arrives.
  • Photography + Private Exclusive build demand before day one on market.
  • Price with data. Overpricing costs more than it ever earns.

The bottom line.

Top-dollar results in Los Angeles don't happen by accident. They happen because a seller and their agent made good decisions 90 days before anyone saw the home — about what to fix, what to disclose, how to stage, how to price, and how to sequence the marketing. The good news: most sellers don't do this. Which means the ones who do have a meaningful, measurable advantage.

FAQ

Before you list, answered.

What should I do before listing my house in Los Angeles?

Start about 90 days out. First (days 90–60): pull your permit history, order a preliminary title report, and decide on Compass Concierge or a contractor. Next (days 60–30): complete deferred maintenance, run your Natural Hazard Disclosure report, begin staging, and audit your HOA. Finally (days 30–0): book professional photography, use the Private Exclusive window, and price with data.

How far in advance should I prepare to sell my home?

About 90 days. Good contractors and stagers in LA are booked weeks out, retroactive permitting and title issues take time to resolve, and improvements need to be finished before photography. Sellers who start 90 days ahead reach the market better prepared — and get stronger results.

Does staging a home in LA actually increase the sale price?

Yes. Staged homes in Los Angeles consistently sell faster and at higher prices than unstaged equivalents at the same price point, with a reliably positive ROI — typically $3–5 returned for every $1 spent on furniture and design. Book stagers early; they're often booked 3–4 weeks out.

Why does unpermitted work matter when selling in LA?

Unpermitted square footage, garage conversions, or ADUs are one of the most common and expensive surprises in an LA transaction. Pull your permit records before a buyer's inspector does so you can disclose and price accordingly, permit retroactively, or convert it back — none of which are fast decisions.

When is the best time to list a home in Los Angeles?

Spring (February through May) historically sees the highest buyer activity, and Thursday is the strongest day to go live so your listing is freshest for weekend showings. That said, well-priced inventory moves in every season — don't wait for the "perfect time" if your home is ready now.
