The LA Home Seller Checklist: The 90 Days Before You List.

The sellers who get the best prices in Los Angeles aren't the ones who picked the right week to list. They're the ones who spent the 90 days before listing doing things most sellers never do. Here's the playbook, in order.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · February 19, 2026 · 5 min read

Price still matters. Presentation still matters. But the foundation of a top-dollar result is built long before anyone ever sees your home. The most expensive decisions a seller makes — what to fix, what to disclose, how to stage, how to price, and how to sequence the marketing — all have lead times. Handle them on a 90-day runway and you control the outcome. Handle them in the final two weeks and you react to it.

The 90-day runway, in three phases.

Every task below is sequenced backward from your list date. The point isn't to do everything at once — it's to start the slow, high-impact items early so nothing forces a rushed, value-losing decision at the end.

Days 90–60: the decisions that affect your net.

Pull your permit history. Unpermitted work is one of the most common — and most expensive — surprises in a Los Angeles transaction. Pull your own records through the city or county portal before a buyer's inspector does. Unpermitted square footage, a garage conversion, or an ADU built without permits leaves you three options — disclose and price accordingly, permit retroactively, or convert it back — and none are fast. You need 90 days to handle it properly.

Order a preliminary title report. Liens, clouds on title, and easement issues surface here. A pre-listing title review costs roughly $150–$200 and can save weeks of escrow delays. If there's anything unusual, you want to know before buyers do.

Decide on Compass Concierge or your own contractor. If your home needs work — kitchen refresh, paint, landscaping, flooring — you have about 75 days from here to finish before photography. The Compass Concierge program funds eligible improvements upfront with no interest, repaid at closing. Going the independent route? Get bids now — good contractors in LA are booked weeks out.

Get a pre-listing appraisal on higher-value homes. For homes likely to attract jumbo financing, a pre-listing appraisal sets realistic price expectations and arms you with documentation if a buyer's appraisal comes in low. It's not always necessary, but it's almost always useful above $2 million.

Days 60–30: preparation and positioning.

Complete all deferred maintenance. This sounds obvious and almost never gets done properly. The checklist: HVAC service and a tune-up if due, a roof inspection and minor repairs, a water-heater age check (buyers and lenders flag units over 10 years), exterior caulking and paint touch-ups, and a full check of every GFCI, smoke detector, and CO detector. California law requires working smoke and CO detectors at transfer — a $15 item that shows up on every inspection report if missed.

Run your NHD report. Natural Hazard Disclosure reports are required in California and cover fire zones, flood zones, earthquake-fault proximity, and other mandatory disclosures. LA County has expanded high-fire-hazard zones significantly over the past five years. Know your zone before a buyer sees it in the report.

Begin the staging conversation. Good stagers in LA are booked 3–4 weeks out for occupied homes, longer for vacant ones. Start now, even if you're undecided. Staged homes consistently sell faster and at higher prices than unstaged equivalents at the same price point — the ROI on professional staging is reliably positive, typically $3–5 returned for every $1 spent.

Audit your HOA, if applicable. Pull current financials, the past 12 months of meeting minutes, and any pending special assessments. These become part of the buyer's due diligence, and surprises — a large assessment, active litigation, a reserve fund below 10% — kill deals or force price reductions. Better to know now.

Key takeaways

  • Start 90 days out — the highest-impact items (permits, title, improvements, staging) all have lead times.
  • Days 90–60 protect your net; Days 60–30 protect the deal; Days 30–0 protect your price.
  • Professional photography and the Compass Private Exclusive window compound your first impression.
  • Price with data, not sentiment — an overpriced LA listing usually closes below a correctly priced one.
  • List on a Thursday for maximum weekend freshness.

Days 30–0: the launch.

Professional photography is non-negotiable. Roughly 97% of buyers start their search online — the photos are the first showing. Budget for still photography, a video walkthrough, and 3D Matterport if the property is over $1.5M or has a complex layout. Drone photography earns its cost for views, large lots, or notable outdoor space.

The Private Exclusive window. Before public MLS launch, Compass's Private Exclusive program markets your home to 34,000+ Compass agents — generating early interest, gathering intelligence on price sensitivity, and creating urgency before days-on-market starts counting. This phase typically runs 1–2 weeks and should be used on virtually every listing.

Price with data, not sentiment. The most expensive mistake sellers make is pricing to what they want rather than what the data supports. An overpriced LA listing typically sits 30+ days, accumulates a "stale" signal, and closes below where a correctly priced listing would have — with more stress and more time invested. Insist on a written CMA with genuine comparables, not a number designed to win the listing.

Time the market week, not just the season. In LA, Thursday is the strongest day to go live — buyers search over the weekend and your listing peaks for Saturday showings. Spring (February–May) sees the highest buyer activity, but well-priced inventory moves in every season. And once you're under contract, negotiation is its own skill — see how to get the right offer.

Top-dollar results in Los Angeles don't happen by accident. They happen because a seller and their agent made good decisions 90 days before anyone saw the home.

The bottom line.

The good news is that most sellers don't do this. Which means the ones who do have a meaningful, measurable advantage — before a single buyer walks through the door. If you're thinking about selling in the next year, the best time to start the checklist is now.
