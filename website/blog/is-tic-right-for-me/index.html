Is a TIC right for you?

Tenancy-in-common ownership is the most under-used affordable path into Los Angeles real estate — typically 15–30% below a comparable condo. Here's how a TIC works, the honest pros and cons, and how to know if it fits your goals.

Michael Abraham
Principal Agent, Architect · Mar 13, 2026 · 5 min read

If you're looking to buy in Los Angeles, you've probably noticed that home prices are… well, a little expensive. But there's a clever workaround that doesn't involve winning the lottery or moving to Barstow: tenancy in common (TIC). TICs are gaining traction in high-cost cities like LA and San Francisco — so what is a TIC, and how do you know if it's right for you? Let's break it down, minus the jargon headache.

What is a TIC?

A tenancy in common is a shared-ownership structure. Instead of owning an entire building or an individually deeded unit (like a condo), you own a percentage of the property but get the exclusive right to occupy a specific unit. You live in your unit like it's all yours — without paying the full "this-is-all-mine" price. Because TICs skip the slow, costly condo-conversion process, they sell for less. That price gap is the whole point.

Why buyers choose a TIC in Los Angeles.

  • More affordable than condos or homes. TICs usually sell for 15–30% less than condos in the same neighborhood — real money, not "skip-a-latte" savings.
  • Own in prime locations. Want to live in Santa Monica, Silver Lake, Venice, or West Hollywood without dropping $1M+? A TIC can be your ticket to a great ZIP code without the financial anxiety.
  • Lower property taxes. Thanks to Prop 13, many LA TICs keep a lower original tax base — so you're not gouged every time the market spikes.
  • Smarter, flexible financing. Fractional financing means your own mortgage — no shared debt and no awkward conversations with co-owners when rates move.
  • Great for investors and house-hackers. Want into the multi-unit game without buying a full fourplex? A TIC is a low-barrier way in, with long-term upside for owner-occupants.

How TIC financing works.

This is the question that used to kill TIC deals — and the reason the market has grown. Today most buyers use a fractional loan: an individual mortgage tied only to your share, so you're never on the hook for a co-owner's payments. A handful of LA lenders specialize in it, offering up to 85% LTV, interest-only, and bank-statement options.

Okay — but what's the catch?

TICs aren't all sunshine and avocado toast. Here's what to watch before you commit:

  • Read the TIC agreement carefully — it's your real estate prenup.
  • Not every TIC is eligible for conventional loans; some require higher down payments or creative financing.
  • The resale market is growing but still isn't fully mainstream — though awareness in LA is climbing fast.
A fractional TIC loan means you finance your unit alone — the single biggest reason tenancy-in-common ownership is now a realistic path to buying in Los Angeles.

So — should you TIC it or skip it?

If you're priced out of LA's traditional market but still want to build equity, grow wealth, and stop paying your landlord's mortgage, a TIC might be your real-estate life hack. The right answer depends on the specific building, the agreement, and your financing — exactly the diligence we run on every TIC. Key takeaways

  • A TIC is a share of one deed plus the exclusive right to occupy your unit — cheaper than a comparable condo by 15–30%.
  • Fractional loans finance your share only, up to 85% LTV — no shared debt with co-owners.
  • Prop 13 often means a lower property-tax base than a new purchase would carry.
  • Read the TIC agreement, confirm financing eligibility, and factor in a smaller resale pool.
  • With the right building and agreement, a TIC is a legitimate path to LA homeownership.
