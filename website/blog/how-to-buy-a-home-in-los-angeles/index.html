How to Buy a Home in Los Angeles.

The complete 2026 process — pre-approval, the offer, escrow, the 17 and 21 day contingency periods, closing costs at common LA price points, and the 30-day sprint from acceptance to keys. Written for buyers who want to know what's actually going to happen.

The AMRE Team
AMRE Real Estate Group at Compass · May 27, 2026 · 9 min read

If you're thinking about buying a home in Los Angeles, the process can sound more complicated than it is. The good news is that it's standardized — the same C.A.R. contract template runs more than 95% of residential California sales, escrow companies handle the same closing choreography every day, and the timeline has well-defined milestones with concrete deadlines. The not-so-good news: Los Angeles has a handful of local specifics — a city transfer tax, Measure ULA mansion-tax thresholds at the top of the market, active fire zones in the hills, rent-control rules on multi-unit purchases — that nobody tells first-time buyers about until those things start mattering.

This is the plain-language walkthrough: what happens from "I'm thinking about buying" to keys in hand, what it actually costs at common LA price points, and where the moments are that decide whether the transaction goes smoothly or sideways.

Step 1 — Get pre-approved before you tour.

The single most useful thing you can do before walking your first open house is to get a real pre-approval from a lender — not a pre-qualification, which is just a soft conversation about what you might qualify for. A pre-approval means the lender has pulled credit, verified income and assets, and issued a letter saying they will lend you a specific amount on specific terms, subject only to appraisal and final underwriting.

Two practical notes for LA buyers. First, ask the lender whether your scenario is conforming (loan amount within the conforming limit, which for high-cost LA County in 2026 is generally up to roughly $1.2M) or jumbo (above that). Jumbo loans have different reserve requirements, often slightly higher rates, and a different underwriting cadence — knowing which bucket you're in shapes the entire offer strategy. Second, get the letter in writing in dollars; a "letter for up to $X" is far less useful to a seller than one sized at your offer price.

While you're at it, ask for a fee worksheet so you can see the lender side of closing costs — origination, points, processing, underwriting, prepaid interest, escrow reserves. Those items alone typically run 1% to 2% of the loan amount before you add escrow, title, and recording.

Step 2 — The LA search (including the part Zillow doesn't show you).

The public search is straightforward: Zillow, Redfin, and Realtor.com all pull from the MLS feed for whichever Los Angeles MLS covers the area (CRMLS for most of LA County, The MLS / CLAW for parts of the Westside). What you see there is the active, on-market inventory.

What you don't see is the off-market layer — Compass Private Exclusives, brokerage pocket listings, and pre-market homes. In neighborhoods like Beverly Hills, Bel Air, Brentwood, Carbon Beach, and Point Dume, off-market inventory can carry an outsized share of the homes most worth seeing. If you're working with an agent inside one of the major networks, you should be getting that feed automatically. If you're not, you're searching from half a deck. Neighborhood selection is its own conversation — pick by life, not by house.

Step 3 — Writing the offer.

Once you find the home, the offer is written on the California Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA), the same form used across virtually all residential sales statewide. The terms a buyer's agent will help you set:

01

Get pre-approved

Credit, income, and assets verified; a dollar-sized letter in hand and a fee worksheet reviewed before you tour.

02

Write the offer

Price, earnest money, down payment and loan type, contingency periods, close date, and any credits — all negotiable on the RPA.

03

Open escrow

On acceptance, the neutral clock starts: EMD in ~3 days, inspections, appraisal, loan, and the final walkthrough before keys.

The negotiable levers in more detail: Price, anchored by comparable recent sales, days on market, and the seller's situation. Earnest Money Deposit, typically 1–3% of price, due to escrow within about 3 business days of acceptance, credited toward your down payment at closing. Down payment and loan terms, including loan type (conventional, jumbo, FHA, VA). Contingency periods, default 17/17/21 days for inspection, appraisal, and loan — often shortened to 10/10/14 or less in competitive situations. Close-of-escrow date, customarily 30 days for financed offers and 14–21 for cash. And seller credits, often left blank in the offer and negotiated after inspection. The seller then accepts, counters, or rejects — and most offers get countered at least once.

Los Angeles adds its own line items. There's a city documentary transfer tax, and at the top of the market the Measure ULA "mansion tax" applies above its thresholds — though that's typically a seller cost, it shapes pricing and negotiation. If you're buying in the hills, expect fire-zone insurance and disclosure requirements; if you're buying a multi-unit property, rent-control and tenant rules come into play. A local agent flags these before they become surprises.

Key takeaways

  • Get a real, dollar-sized pre-approval before you tour — and know if you're conforming or jumbo.
  • The best homes often trade off-market; make sure you're seeing the full deck.
  • Offers are written on the California RPA — price is one lever among many.
  • Default contingencies are 17/17/21 days; a financed deal closes in ~30–45.
  • Budget 2–5% of price for closing costs, plus LA-specific taxes and disclosures.

Frequently Asked Questions.

How long does it take to buy a home in Los Angeles?

A typical financed purchase runs about 30 to 45 days from accepted offer to keys. Cash can close in roughly 7 to 14 days. The default California contingency timeline is 17 days for inspection, 17 for appraisal, and 21 for loan, though competitive offers often shorten these.

How much are closing costs for a buyer in LA?

Typically about 2% to 5% of the purchase price, on top of your down payment — lender fees (often 1–2% of the loan), escrow and title, recording, and prepaid interest and reserves. LA buyers should also account for the city transfer tax and, at the top of the market, Measure ULA thresholds.

Do I need a pre-approval before touring homes?

Effectively yes for a competitive LA offer. A pre-approval is a lender letter issued after they verify credit, income, and assets. It's stronger than a pre-qualification — get it in writing and sized to your offer price before you tour.

What's the difference between a conforming and a jumbo loan in LA?

Conforming is at or below the limit — for high-cost LA County in 2026, generally up to roughly $1.2M. Above that is jumbo, which usually carries higher reserve requirements, sometimes higher rates, and a different underwriting cadence. Which bucket you're in shapes your whole offer strategy.

Is earnest money refundable?

Earnest money is typically 1–3% of price, deposited to escrow within about 3 business days of acceptance and credited toward your down payment. While your contingencies are active you can generally cancel and recover it; once contingencies are removed, it can be at risk if you breach the contract.
