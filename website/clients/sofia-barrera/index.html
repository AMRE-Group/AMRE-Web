Each of these is a beautiful listing. But beauty is the easy part — what matters is what each one will be like to own. Here's what I see in each, including the things only an architect tends to flag.

What I heard from your picks

You gravitate toward expansive glass, dramatic volume, and panoramic views — the architecture of openness. Two are full-service Wilshire Corridor high-rises; one is a private, ground-up Bel Air estate. Same instinct, three very different ways to live it.

A two-level penthouse with theatrical 25-ft ceilings, a sculptural spiral staircase, floor-to-ceiling glass, three fireplaces, and an in-unit elevator. Griffith-Observatory-to-Getty views from nearly every room. Resort pool, rare tennis court, sauna. Built 1989 · HOA $6,463/mo.

◆ The Architect's Eye

That 25-foot volume is breathtaking — and vertical drama has trade-offs an architect reads instantly: acoustics, HVAC zoning, and how cleanly that in-unit elevator was integrated (it's your resale insurance for two-story living). I'll verify the curtain-wall and flue conditions; in an '89 building those are the line items that quietly move a number.

It's been listed 178 days with a recent price cut — that's real negotiating room. I'll pressure-test the per-foot against the building's true comps, not the headline.

This is the only true architectural blank check of the three — a 2022 ground-up build means current code, modern systems, and disappearing glass walls already engineered, with far less risk than retrofitting drama into an '89 tower. The "space allocated for a future elevator" is a tell: the architect of record planned for aging-in-place and resale, and that rough-in is worth real money.

160 ft of knoll frontage is that rare pairing of presence and privacy. I'll read the grading, setbacks, and motorcourt against the lot so we know exactly what's protected — and what's expandable.
